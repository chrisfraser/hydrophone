Calibration: Giving Your Hydrophone a Real Number

You built a serious instrument in B3. Until you calibrate it, it's only a microphone — it tells you something happened, not how loud. Today you turn recorded volts into real pressure.

⏱ ~15 minutes• 🧮 One equation, one calculator• 🔬 Needs the B3 build (or a bought hydrophone)• Make it measure
◎ Why this lesson

Every quantitative claim in this course runs through one number: your hydrophone's sensitivity. Source levels ("that click was 225 dB at the source"), the sonar equation, the confidence of a localization fix — all of it assumes you can convert a recorded voltage into an honest pressure in µPa. Calibration is what earns you that number. Skip it and every decibel you report is a guess.

1From "microphone" to instrument

Your B3 build hears beautifully. But a recording is just a stream of voltages, and a voltage on its own means nothing — was that 0.3 V click a faint whale far off, or a loud one through an insensitive element? You can't say until you know how many volts this exact hydrophone produces per micropascal of pressure. That conversion factor is its receiving sensitivity, M, measured in dB re 1 V/µPa.2

Recall the sign trap from T2: sensitivity is always a negative number, and less-negative means more sensitive. A hydrophone at −165 dB re 1 V/µPa puts out far more voltage for the same sound than one at −185 dB.2 The research-grade Cetacean C57, for instance, is rated −187 dB re 1 V/µPa at the element, lifted to about −167 or −154 dB once its +20 or +33 dB preamp is switched in.6 Calibration is simply the act of pinning down that number for your unit — because nominal datasheet figures and the device on your bench are never quite the same.

2The one equation: volts → pressure

Here is the whole job in a single line. Given a recorded voltage, you recover the sound pressure level that produced it:1

SPL = 20·log₁₀( V / Vref ) − M − G V = measured voltage · Vref = 1 V · M = sensitivity (dB re 1 V/µPa, negative) · G = preamp/recorder gain (dB) · result in dB re 1 µPa

Read it left to right. 20·log₁₀(V/Vref) expresses your recorded voltage in dB relative to one volt (a "dBV"). You then subtract the sensitivity — and because M is negative, subtracting it adds a big positive number, which is what lifts a tiny voltage up to a realistic 150-plus-dB pressure. Finally you subtract any gain G you dialled in, because that amplification was added by your electronics, not by the whale.1

★ Work one by hand

A click peaks at V = 0.50 V. Your hydrophone is M = −180 dB re 1 V/µPa and you recorded with G = 20 dB of gain. Then 20·log₁₀(0.50) = −6.0, so SPL = −6.0 − (−180) − 20 = 154 dB re 1 µPa. That single number is the difference between "I heard a click" and a measurement you can put in a logbook. Now play with it below.

3How calibration actually gets done

There are three ways to attach a real number to a hydrophone. Only one is realistic on a hobbyist budget — but it's worth knowing what the gold standard is, because that's what your reference unit was calibrated against.

Calibration methods, hardest-and-best first. The standard for the field is IEC 60565-1.3
MethodWhat it isWho does it
Free-field reciprocity
primary · absolute		Three transducers, spherical-wave maths, no pre-calibrated reference needed. SI-traceable.National metrology labs (NPL and peers)
Comparison
secondary · practical		Put your unit beside a calibrated reference in the same sound field; transfer its known number.You, on the bench — and accredited labs
Pistonphone
absolute · one frequency		A known pressure inside a sealed coupler at a single low frequency — a fast spot-check, not a full curve.Quick field/bench verification

Reciprocity is the primary method: it derives sensitivity from first principles, which is why it lives in a handful of national labs.3 Everything else is secondary — it transfers that lab-grade truth to a working device. Accredited free-field calibration reaches uncertainties well under 1 dB (often a few tenths), which is the bar a serious measurement chain is held to.4

Your route is comparison calibration: borrow or buy a calibrated reference hydrophone — an Aquarian H2a with a cal sheet, or a borrowed lab unit7 — and read both devices in one sound field. The reference tells you the true pressure; your recording tells you the voltage; the ratio gives you M.

  1. Get a calibrated reference into your hands
    A hydrophone with a current calibration certificate — its sensitivity is the "known" you'll transfer onto your own build.
    Tip: a buffered H2a with a cal sheet is the cheapest honest reference most hobbyists can reach.7
  2. Put both in the same sound field
    Suspend the reference and your unit close together in a tank or quiet water, facing a steady tone source. The goal is for both to see the same pressure.
    Tip: keep the spacing small versus the wavelength so neither sits in a different part of the wave.
  3. Record both, then back out the pressure
    From the reference's known sensitivity and its recorded voltage, compute the true SPL in the tank. That pressure is now your "truth" value.
    Tip: sweep a few frequencies — a single tone hides resonances your element may have.
  4. Solve for your sensitivity M
    Rearrange the equation: your unit saw the same SPL, so M = 20·log₁₀(V/Vref) − SPL − G. Record that number; it is your calibration.
    Tip: redo it every 3–5 years — sensitivity drifts with age, handling and water.5
⚠ Be honest: your B1 disc can't really be calibrated

This lesson is for the serious build. A bare $5 piezo disc is resonant, bass-rolled and wildly variable unit-to-unit — two discs from the same bag don't share a sensitivity, and a sharp resonant peak means a single number can't describe its response anyway. You can characterise its shape for fun, but treating a cheap disc as a calibrated instrument is self-deception. Calibrate the well-built, flat, repeatable instrument — that's the one whose number you can trust.

4Why this matters for the mission

A calibrated hydrophone is the entire difference between a recording and sperm-whale science. With a real M you can finally do the things this course has been promising:

  • Report a source level. Measure the received level, add back the transmission loss over the range, and you can state "that click was ~225 dB re 1 µPa @ 1 m at the source" — a number, not an impression.
  • Trust your detection-range maths. The passive sonar equation (built in T7) needs a real received level to predict how far you can hear a whale.8 Feed it a fictional voltage and the answer is fiction.
  • Believe your localization. A position fix from an array is only as solid as the levels and timings feeding it — uncalibrated elements quietly poison the confidence of every fix.
