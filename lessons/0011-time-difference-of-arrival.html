Time Difference of Arrival: Cross-Correlation & Bearings

One click, two hydrophones, two slightly different arrival times. That tiny gap is the single measurement every position fix is built from — here is what it is, how you read it, and what it can and can't tell you.

⏱ ~14 minutes• 🧮 Balanced math + one toy• Mission: two ears → a compass
◎ Why this lesson

By now you can detect a sperm-whale click on a single channel (T8). Detection answers "is a whale clicking?" — it says nothing about where. Localization is just detection plus one extra ingredient: timing. This lesson introduces the measurement the entire summit of the course rests on — the time difference of arrival between two hydrophones — and turns your pair of ears into a compass that points at a clicking Mauritius whale.

1The clock, not the loudness

The same click leaves the whale once, but it does not reach two separated hydrophones at the same instant. If the whale is a little closer to hydrophone 1, the sound arrives there first; the second hydrophone hears the identical click a fraction of a millisecond later. That sliver of delay is the time difference of arrival (TDOA) — written Δt.

TDOA is powerful because it converts cleanly into geometry. Sound travels at a known speed c (~1500 m/s in seawater). So the extra time is just extra distance: multiply the delay by c and you get the difference in path length from the source to the two hydrophones.1

Δd = c · Δt Δd = path-length difference (m) · c = sound speed (~1500 m/s) · Δt = time difference of arrival (s)
★ A worked example — burn this in

Suppose the click hits hydrophone 1 exactly 0.4 ms before hydrophone 2. Convert the delay to a distance:

Δd = 1500 m/s × 0.0004 s = 0.6 m

The source is 0.6 m farther from hydrophone 2 than from hydrophone 1 — no more, no less. Notice what you don't get: not the range, not the position. One TDOA fixes a difference of distances, and "all points whose distance to two fixed points differs by a constant" is the textbook definition of a hyperbola. Your source lies somewhere on that curve.

2How you actually measure Δt — cross-correlation

You can't eyeball the delay; a click is a few hundred microseconds long and buried in noise. The standard tool is cross-correlation: slide one channel's waveform across the other and, at each lag, multiply-and-sum to score how well they overlap. The lag that scores highest is the delay. As PAMGuard puts it, TDOA is "most often made by finding the peak in the cross correlation function of the signal waveform recorded at each hydrophone."1

Raw correlation works, but in a noisy, echoey ocean the peak can smear into a broad, ambiguous lump. The fix is GCC-PHAT — Generalized Cross-Correlation with Phase Transform. Before correlating, it phase-whitens both signals: it divides each frequency by its own magnitude, throwing away how loud each frequency is and keeping only when it arrived (its phase).2 Whitening flattens the spectrum so every frequency votes equally on the timing, collapsing the correlation into a single sharp spike — close to a Dirac delta — right at the true delay, even under reverberation and noise.2 The method dates to Knapp & Carter (1976) and is still the workhorse of acoustic time-delay estimation.34

⌗ Why "whiten"?

A loud, low-frequency rumble can dominate plain correlation and drag the peak around. Phase-whitening removes the amplitude bias so a faint broadband click and a loud tonal both contribute the same timing information. The trade: PHAT is brilliant for timing but discards level — which is exactly why we use it for TDOA and a different tool for measuring loudness.

3Two ears give a direction, not a place

Here is the humbling part. A single TDOA pins the whale to a whole hyperbola, and far from the array that hyperbola straightens into a ray — a bearing. The bearing angle θ, measured from the line perpendicular to your two-hydrophone baseline (broadside), follows directly from the path difference and the spacing B:

sin θ = Δd / B = c·Δt / B θ = bearing off broadside · Δd = path difference · B = hydrophone spacing (baseline)

But a direction is not a position, and worse, it is a doubly ambiguous direction. With only two hydrophones you measure one number, so you can resolve only one dimension — PAMGuard is blunt about it: two sensors give "a bearing, but it will be ambiguous."5 A source on one side of the baseline and its mirror-image twin on the other side are exactly the same distance from each hydrophone, so they produce the identical TDOA. Two ears cannot tell them apart — the famous left/right ambiguity. ⚠ Don't over-claim from a pair

A two-hydrophone bearing is genuinely useful — it tells you which way to point the boat — but it is not a fix. You don't know how far, and you can't yet say left or right of the line. Resolving that needs more sensors: three break the left/right tie in a plane, and five well-spread hydrophones give an unambiguous 3-D position.5 That is precisely what T11 builds — crossing several bearings/hyperbolas into a single point.8

4Sound speed: the conversion factor — and the main error

Every step above multiplied by c. That single number is the bridge from time (which you measure beautifully) to distance (which you actually want) — so any error in c propagates straight into your geometry. And c is not a constant. In the ocean it runs roughly 1450–1570 m/s, rising with temperature (~+4 m/s per °C), salinity (~+1.3 m/s per psu) and pressure (~+1.7 m/s per 100 m of depth).6 Because warm surface water is faster than the cold deep, sound rays bend — and that bending, captured in the sound-speed profile (SSP), is the dominant source of localization error.6

For a first cut you'll use c ≈ 1500 m/s and accept a few-percent wobble. To do better you measure (or model) the SSP for the western Mauritius slope and ray-trace — a refinement we revisit once the array is up.

What you can now say

  1. The same click reaches two hydrophones at slightly different times — the TDOA, the atom of localization.
  2. Δd = c · Δt: delay × sound speed = path-length difference, which constrains the source to a hyperbola.
  3. You measure Δt by the peak of the cross-correlation; GCC-PHAT phase-whitens to make that peak sharp under noise and reverb.
  4. Two hydrophones give a bearing (sin θ = Δd / B), not a position — and it carries a left/right ambiguity.
  5. Sound speed (~1500 m/s) is the time→distance converter and the main error source; it varies with temperature, salinity and depth.
