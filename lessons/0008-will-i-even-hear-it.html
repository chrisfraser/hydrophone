Will I Even Hear It? The Passive Sonar Equation

One equation answers the question every deployment hinges on: at range R, does a clicking whale rise above the sea's noise — or vanish into it? Let's assemble it, term by term.

⏱ ~14 minutes• 🧮 Balanced math — the central calculation• Bring T3 decibels & T1/T5 source level
◎ Why this lesson

Back in T1 a toy calculator gave you a first, rough taste of "how far can I hear a whale?" — a single loudness-minus-distance subtraction. That was training wheels. This lesson does the calculation properly: the full passive sonar equation, with the noise of the sea, your sensor's directivity, and a detection threshold all in their right places. It is the feasibility check before any mooring goes in the water, and the backbone of the array design we reach in T11.

1The one equation — and why "passive" makes it simpler

A sonar equation is just honest bookkeeping in decibels: start with how loud the source is, subtract everything that works against you, add the few things that work for you, and see if anything is left over. Because you only listen (you never ping), your version drops two complications that the active, ping-and-listen equation has to carry.2

SE = ( SL − TL ) − ( NL − DI ) − DT Signal excess SE in dB. Detect the whale when SE > 0. Every term is a level or a loss in decibels.
SL
Source level — how loud the click is, referred to 1 m from the whale (you already have this from T1/T5).
TL
Transmission loss — how much the click weakens travelling range R to your hydrophone.
NL
Noise level — the background hiss of the sea in the click's band, the floor a signal must beat.
DI
Directivity index — how much a directional array suppresses noise off its look direction.
DT
Detection threshold — the signal-to-noise margin your detector needs to call it a real click.
⌗ Two things the active equation has that yours doesn't

An active sonar pings, the sound hits a target, and an echo comes back. So its equation carries a target-strength term (how reflective the target is) and two-way transmission loss (out and back, so 2·TL). Passive listening has neither: the whale is the source, not a reflector, and its sound makes the trip once. That is the whole reason a thumb-sized hydrophone can hear a whale kilometres off — you spend your decibel budget on one trip, not a round one.12

2SL and TL — the loud part, then the long swim

SL is the gift. A sperm whale's usual click leaves the source at roughly 230 dB re 1 µPa @ 1 m (on-axis levels up to ~236 dB have been measured) — the loudest sound made by any animal.7 That enormous starting number is the budget you get to spend on distance.

TL is the bill. Sound spreads out from the whale like an expanding sphere; the same energy smeared over a bigger and bigger surface means it gets quieter with range. Near the source the loss is spherical:

TL = 20 · log₁₀( r ) + α · rkm r in metres. The 20·log₁₀ r term is spherical spreading — ≈ 6 dB lost per doubling of range. The α·r term is absorption: seawater itself soaks up the high frequencies, ~1 dB per km near a click's ~15 kHz.

Far out — once the click is trapped between the sea surface and the seabed and can no longer spread downward — spreading eases to cylindrical, TL = 10·log₁₀ r, only ≈ 3 dB per doubling.3 For the few-kilometre ranges we care about off Mauritius, spherical spreading plus a little absorption is the honest model, and it's what the calculator below uses.9

3NL, DI, DT — the floor you must clear

Subtracting TL from SL gives the received level (RL) at your hydrophone. But "received" isn't "heard" — the click has to out-shout the ocean.

NL — the sea is never silent. Distant shipping rumbles at low frequencies; wind and breaking waves hiss across the mid and high band; rain and snapping shrimp add their own texture. The standard summary is the Wenz curves, which map noise level against frequency and sea state.56 A calm night might sit near 65 dB in the click band; a rough, ship-busy day pushes well past 85 dB. Higher sea state, smaller detection range — the noise slider below is the single biggest day-to-day variable you don't control.

DI — what an array buys you. A single omnidirectional hydrophone listens equally in all directions, so it can't tell the whale's sound from noise arriving elsewhere: DI ≈ 0 dB. Point several hydrophones the same way (a steered array) and the noise from other directions partially cancels, adding real gain — that's the prize T11 chases. For now, building one ear, leave DI at zero.111

DT — how sure must you be? The detection threshold is the signal-to-noise margin your detector demands before it's willing to say "click". A confident automatic detector typically wants the signal a handful of decibels above the noise — say ~10 dB for a clean single-click call.4 Lower DT catches fainter clicks but invents more false ones.

4A worked example — a whale 10 km out

Let's do it by hand, then let the calculator do it live. Pick a usual click (SL = 230), put the whale 10 km away, a calm-ish sea (NL = 70), one omni hydrophone (DI = 0), and a sensible detector (DT = 10).

TL = 20·log₁₀(10 000) + 1·10 = 80 + 10 = 90 dB RL = SL − TL = 230 − 90 = 140 dB re 1 µPa floor = NL − DI + DT = 70 − 0 + 10 = 80 dB SE = RL − floor = 140 − 80 = +60 dB SE = +60 dB is hugely positive — at 10 km, an on-axis usual click isn't just detectable, it's thunderous. Setting SE = 0 and solving for range gives an ideal maximum of roughly 55 km. Hold that number lightly — the next callout explains why reality is far stingier.

Sixty decibels of headroom sounds too good to be true, and in a sense it is: this is the best-case ceiling, the number you'd get if the whale aimed its click straight at you in calm, simple water. It tells you the mission is comfortably feasible — but not that you'll hear every whale at 55 km. Read the caveat, then play.

★ The honest caveats (same spirit as T1)

The equation gives an upper bound, not a promise. Three things shrink it hard in the field:

  • The click is a beam. A usual click is a tight forward-pointing searchlight; off to the side it is 20–30 dB quieter. A whale clicking away from you can lose more range than any amount of noise.8
  • The water bends sound. Refraction and shadow zones mean the simple spreading curve is an idealisation; real paths can skip right over your sensor.
  • Detecting ≠ locating. One faint click is a detection; pinning a 3-D position needs several clean clicks at several sensors. A real Mauritius-style observatory localises sperm whales out to about 7 km — that, not 55, is the number to design an array around.10

What you can now say

  1. The passive sonar equation is SE = (SL − TL) − (NL − DI) − DT; you detect when SE > 0.
  2. Passive listening drops the target-strength term and uses one-way TL — that's why it's so forgiving.
  3. TL = 20·log₁₀ r (spherical, ~6 dB/doubling) plus ~1 dB/km absorption, easing to 10·log₁₀ r when ducted.
  4. NL rises with sea state (Wenz); DI ≈ 0 for one omni ear; DT is your required SNR.
  5. The equation gives an upper bound — beam pattern, refraction and localization needs make real ranges far shorter (~7 km for fixes).
