Pressure → Voltage: How a Hydrophone Works

You built a $5 disc and it made a tiny voltage. Now meet the mechanism that turns a passing sound wave into electricity — the one mental model that makes preamps, sensitivity and calibration make sense.

⏱ ~10 minutes• 🧠 Theory — no build required• Mission: detect & localize
◎ Why this lesson

In B1 you soldered a piezo disc and it twitched the meters. But why? This lesson opens the black box. Once you can picture a sound wave squeezing a ceramic and squirting out charge, three things you'll meet later stop being magic: why the element needs a preamp, what a hydrophone's sensitivity number means, and how recorded volts become real pressure. This physics is exactly why a cheap disc can register a whale's click — and why, for the real mission, you'll later pick a sensitive PZT cylinder and a proper preamp to actually hear a sperm whale.

1The trick: squeeze a ceramic, get electricity

A hydrophone's sensing core is a piezoelectric ceramic — almost always PZT (lead zirconate titanate). Piezoelectric materials produce small electric charges when a pressure change flexes them: the element bends under the pressure of a passing sound wave and, in return, gives off an electrical signal.1 That is the whole transducer in one sentence — pressure in, voltage out.

Mechanically, PZT is packed with tiny built-in electric dipoles, frozen into alignment when the ceramic was made. Squeeze the disc and you nudge that lattice; the dipoles shift and a net + charge piles on one face, − on the other. Release it and the charge falls back. Drive it with the rapid push-and-pull of a sound wave and the faces swing positive-negative in lockstep with the pressure — a voltage that is a faithful copy of the sound. (This is the direct piezoelectric effect. Run it backwards — apply a voltage to make the ceramic move — and you'd have a projector that emits sound. A hydrophone only listens.)

★ This is a passive transducer — no power required

The energy in the output comes from the sound itself, not from a battery. A hydrophone listens and converts acoustic energy into electrical energy; it never transmits, and the bare element needs no power at all to work.1 (A preamp needs power — the element does not. Keep those two separate in your head and half the wiring confusion disappears.) That passivity is a gift: drop the disc in the sea, and a 230 dB sperm-whale click does all the work of generating your signal for free.

2The catch: it's a feeble source with a built-in capacitor

The output is real, but it is tiny and comes from a very fussy source. Electrically, a piezo disc behaves like a sound-dependent voltage source in series with about 15 nF of capacitance — for a standard 27 mm disc, ~15 nF.2 That built-in capacitor is the source of the single most common DIY disappointment.

A capacitor and the recorder's input resistance form a high-pass filter. Plug a 15 nF disc into a low-impedance input and the bass is thrown away before it's ever recorded: into a 50 kΩ line input the cut sits around 200 Hz; into a ~7 kΩ phone-mic input it climbs to roughly 1 kHz.2 That's why a bare piezo sounds thin and honky. The fix is to feed it a high-impedance input — and to do that well, the element wants a preamp sitting right beside it.

⤴ Forward ref — this is why B2 exists

You met the symptom in B1 ("borrow a Hi-Z input so the bass survives"). Here's the cause: a 15 nF source impedance that only a high-impedance load can read without strangling the lows. A purpose-built preamp (B2) presents that high impedance and buffers the fragile signal down to a cable-friendly level — the difference between "it kind of works" and a measuring instrument. Hold that thought; we build it next phase.

3Sensitivity: one number for "volts per pressure"

How much voltage does a given hydrophone produce per unit of sound pressure? That single number is its receiving sensitivity, written M and quoted in dB re 1 V/µPa — decibels relative to one volt produced by one micropascal.34 Two quirks trip up everyone:

  • It's always negative. A hydrophone makes a fraction of a volt per µPa, so the conversion factor is a small number and its decibel value comes out below zero.4
  • Less negative = more sensitive. A higher number like −164 dB means more output than a lower one like −180 dB. So −164 dB beats −180 dB — counter-intuitive until it clicks.5

Real hydrophones span a wide range — roughly −154 dB (very sensitive) down to about −204 dB for a budget mini-element.5 Drag along the line below and watch the same sound make wildly different voltages.

4Closing the loop: from volts back to pressure

Sensitivity isn't trivia — it's the key that unlocks measurement. Your recorder hands you volts. To recover the real sound-pressure level that hit the element, you undo the chain: take the recorded voltage, subtract the hydrophone's sensitivity M, and subtract any preamp gain G.4

SPL = 20·log₁₀(V / Vref) − M − G V = recorded voltage · M = receiving sensitivity (dB re 1 V/µPa, negative) · G = system gain (dB). Subtracting a negative M adds level back — exactly the volts the element "lost" turning pressure into signal.
★ Why you can't skip calibration

That formula only works if you know M. A cheap disc's M is unknown and ragged, so it can listen but never measure — fine for hearing boats, useless for a source level. Pin M down (against a reference) and your volts become absolute pascals. That's the whole job of calibration (B4), and it's the line between a microphone and a scientific instrument.

What you can now say

  1. PZT ceramic makes charge when pressure deforms it — pressure in, voltage out, the direct piezoelectric effect.
  2. It's a passive transducer: the sound supplies the energy, the bare element needs no power.
  3. Electrically it's a voltage source in series with ~15 nF, which high-passes the bass into a low-Z input — hence the preamp (B2).
  4. Sensitivity M (dB re 1 V/µPa) is always negative, and less negative = more sensitive (−164 dB beats −180 dB).
  5. Recover real pressure with SPL = 20·log₁₀(V/Vref) − M − G — which is why you must calibrate (B4).
