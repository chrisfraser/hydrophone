Seeing Sound: Waveforms & Spectrograms

The single most-used skill in this whole course is eyeballing a picture of sound. Today you learn to read one — with free software and free recordings, no hardware required.

⏱ ~14 minutes• 🛠 Free software — do this today• Skill: read a spectrogram
◎ Why this lesson

You already know a sperm whale clicks, and you can talk about how loud that click is in decibels. But you have never seen one. Every detection in this course — every position fix, every coda you ever decode — begins the same way: you open a recording and look at a spectrogram. This is where you first lay eyes on a whale click. Learn to read the picture now and the rest of the mission is just practice.

1Two pictures of one sound

A recording is just a long list of numbers — air, or water, pressure measured thousands of times a second. There are two standard ways to draw it, and they answer different questions.2

The waveform plots amplitude against time: a wiggly line, loud bits tall, quiet bits flat. It tells you when something happened and how loud — but nothing about pitch. The spectrogram plots frequency against time, with colour standing in for energy: time runs left-to-right, low notes sit at the bottom and high notes at the top, and a bright pixel means "lots of energy at this pitch, at this instant."1 The waveform shows the shape of the sound; the spectrogram shows its colour. For whales, the spectrogram wins almost every time.

2Why a click is a crisp vertical line

A sperm-whale click is two things at once: extremely brief (a few thousandths of a second) and broadband — its energy is smeared across a huge span of frequencies, from a few hundred hertz up to tens of kilohertz.5 Put those two facts on a frequency-vs-time chart and there is only one shape they can make: a thin stripe that is narrow in time but tall in frequency. A vertical line. That is exactly why a spectrogram is the go-to view for finding clicks — the Audacity manual notes it is "often easier to see clicks" there than in a waveform.3

The opposite kind of sound — a steady whistle or a hum that holds one pitch and lasts a while — does the reverse: narrow in frequency, long in time, so it draws a horizontal line. Keep that pair in your head: brief-and-broadband points up, steady-and-tonal lies flat. Almost everything you will ever hunt for is one of those two shapes, or a smear in between.

⌗ How the spectrogram is actually made

A computer can't ask "what frequencies are here right now" — frequency only has meaning over a stretch of time. So it cheats: it chops the recording into many short, overlapping windows, runs a Fourier transform on each one to find the frequencies inside, and stacks the results side by side into an image. That's the short-time Fourier transform (STFT). The one knob that matters is how long each window is — and, as the next toy shows, it forces a trade you cannot escape.1

3The window-size trade-off — you can't have both

Here is the catch at the heart of every spectrogram. A short window pins down time beautifully (you can say exactly when each click landed) but blurs frequency. A long window pins down frequency beautifully (you can read a tone's pitch to the hertz) but blurs time. Sharpen one and you smear the other — Audacity's own manual shows two clicks that are crisp and separate at a short window "smeared together into one" at a long one.3 There is no setting that is sharp in both at once:

(time resolution) × (frequency resolution) ≈ constant Make the window longer and Δt grows while Δf shrinks — their product barely moves. Pick the window to suit what you're hunting.

Play with the explorer below. It draws the same synthetic sound two ways: a steady tone (the horizontal line) plus a train of broadband clicks (the vertical lines). The waveform up top never changes — it's the raw sound. Only the spectrogram changes, because the window is an analysis choice, not a property of the sound. Switch the window and watch the trade happen.

★ The rule of thumb you'll actually use

Hunting clicks (detection, and later timing the same click at two hydrophones for a position fix)? Use a short window — you need sharp time. Measuring a tone or a whistle's pitch? Use a long window — you need sharp frequency. When you don't yet know what you're looking at, start in the middle and adjust. This one habit carries you straight into sampling (T6) and click detection (T8).

4Do it for real, today

None of this needs your hydrophone. The software is free, the recordings are free and public, and a real sperm-whale click is a few clicks away:

Everything here is free. Open a tool, load a recording, and find a vertical line.
Free toolWhat it's for
AudacityThe everywhere editor. Load a WAV, switch a track to Spectrogram view, set the window size — exactly the knob you just played with.3
Raven Lite (Cornell)Built by bioacousticians for this job: record, and view sound as spectrograms and waveforms. Free for students and hobbyists.4

For something to look at, the Watkins Marine Mammal Sound Database (WHOI) is a free archive of ~2,000 recordings across 60+ species, sperm whales included.6 The Dominica Sperm Whale Project is the home of the canonical coda work and a good window onto what real click trains look like.7 Grab a sperm-whale clip, open it in a spectrogram, and find your first vertical line. That's the win.

What you can now do

  1. Tell a waveform (amplitude vs time) from a spectrogram (frequency vs time, colour = energy) and say what each is good for.
  2. Recognise a sperm-whale click on sight: broadband + brief = a vertical line; a steady tone = a horizontal line.
  3. Explain the STFT window trade-off — short window sharpens time, long window sharpens frequency, never both.
  4. Pick the window for the job: short for clicks, long for tones, middle when unsure.
  5. Practise today with free tools (Audacity, Raven Lite) and free recordings (Watkins, the Dominica project).
