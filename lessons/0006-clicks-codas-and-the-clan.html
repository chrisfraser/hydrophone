Clicks, Codas & the Mauritius Clan

A sperm whale click is a tiny, pneumatic, echo-stuffed event — and the rhythm of a string of them is a name. Learn to read both, and you can pick your whales out of the ocean.

⏱ ~14 minutes• 🛠 No build — listening theory• Mission: identify the clan
◎ Why this lesson

In T1 you learned the whales are loud and that they click. That is enough to detect a sperm whale. It is not enough to say "that's one of ours." This lesson opens up the click — how it's built, why it has structure inside it — and then the coda, the rhythmic click-pattern that carries social identity. By the end you'll know exactly what to listen for to confirm you've found the resident Mauritius clan, and you'll be holding the two clues the rest of the course turns into numbers: head size (T9) and position (T10–T11).

1How a click is actually made

A sperm whale click is not made in a throat. It is made in the animal's nose — a nose that fills a third of its body. Pressurised air is forced past a pair of phonic lips (the museau de singe, or "monkey lips") at the front of the head; they clap shut and fire a sharp pulse of sound backwards into the great waxy spermaceti organ.2 Remarkably, the mechanism keeps working even when a deep dive has crushed the available air to less than 2% of its surface volume — and the same apparatus has a bimodal setting, making clicks tuned either for biosonar or for social calls.2

That backward-fired pulse then ricochets. It reflects off an air sac at the back of the head, runs forward through a fatty lens called the junk, and is beamed out the front — but a portion bounces again and again between the air sacs, so what leaves the whale is not one pulse but a decaying train of pulses, each a round-trip late.1 This is the origin of the click's hidden internal structure, and it is a gift: the gap between those internal pulses — the inter-pulse interval — is set by the length of the head the sound is bouncing in.

⌗ Two intervals — the whole lesson hinges on not confusing them

Inside one click sits the inter-pulse interval (IPI) — a few milliseconds, the echo bouncing across the spermaceti organ. It is a ruler: longer head → longer IPI → bigger whale, via T = 1.258·IPI + 5.736 (length in m).9 Between whole clicks sits the inter-click interval (ICI) — tenths of a second to seconds, the rhythm. The IPI sizes the animal (T9); the ICI tells you what the animal is doing, and — strung into a pattern — who it is. Same prefix, opposite jobs. Keep them apart.

2Four kinds of click, told apart by rhythm

You already met the click-type table in T1. The deeper point is that all four are the same broadband click — what changes is the ICI, the rhythm, because rhythm is the whale's way of switching tasks:

  • Usual clicks — steady ~0.5–1 s apart: long-range biosonar while hunting. As the whale closes on squid, the two-way echo time shrinks and it clicks faster.1
  • Creaks / buzzes — clicks blurring into a 5–100 ms "rrrip": the final lunge at prey. Tagged whales average ~17 prey-capture buzzes per deep dive.1
  • Codas — short patterned bursts, clicks ~0.1–0.5 s apart: social, not for hunting. These are the subject of the rest of this lesson.
  • Slow clicks — booming, ~5–8 s apart: made by large roving males, the signature of a big lone bull. Off western Mauritius, those visiting males are catalogued too.7

Line them up by ear and the differences are stark — the explorer below lets you do exactly that. Switch to Click types and watch how a creak is a near-solid smear while a slow click drops a single lonely tick into four whole seconds.

3Codas — a rhythm that means something

A coda is a brief, deliberate, rhythmic sequence of clicks — typically three to a dozen — exchanged between socialising whales like a call-and-response.1 Crucially, the message is carried by timing, not pitch. Every click is the same broadband snap; what differs is the spacing — and researchers label coda types by that spacing, in notations like "5R" (five regular, evenly spaced), "4+1" (four quick clicks, a pause, then one), or "1+1+3".

How much can mere rhythm encode? A 2024 Project CETI analysis argues: a great deal. Studying a single clan's codas, Sharma and colleagues found they decompose into a small set of features — rhythm (the click pattern), tempo (the overall duration), rubato (smooth tempo-stretching across a back-and-forth), and ornamentation (an extra click tacked on) — which combine into roughly 150 distinguishable codas: a "phonetic alphabet" far larger than anyone had catalogued.3

★ Why "same pitch, different rhythm" is the design you'll exploit

Because identity lives in timing, you don't need a calibrated, flat-response instrument to read a coda — you need good time resolution. Even the cheap piezo disc from B1 resolves the gaps between clicks just fine. Everything that makes a hydrophone expensive (smooth frequency response, low self-noise) matters for sizing and localizing — but to tell your clan from a stranger, a stopwatch on the clicks is most of the job.

4Vocal clans — and the one clan you're hunting

Here is the part that turns biology into a mission plan. Sperm whales that share a coda repertoire — the same dialect of rhythms — form a vocal clan. Clans are not families and not regions: Rendell & Whitehead showed they can be sympatric (overlapping in the same waters) yet stay acoustically distinct, spanning thousands of kilometres and containing thousands of whales, with each unit preferentially associating with its own clan. Their conclusion is striking — culture, not genes or geography, is the main thing structuring sperm-whale society.4 A clan's dialect is learned and passed down the matriline, like an accent.

Off the western slope of Mauritius lives a small resident population — fewer than forty animals, about 38 catalogued by photo-ID since 2011.6 At its core is a stable matrilineal group — "Irène's group", around 28 whales — seen year-round, with its own social units and vocal repertoire.5 In other words: your target isn't "a sperm whale." It's a known clan with a known dialect, at a known address. When your hydrophone catches a coda whose rhythm matches their catalogue, that is the moment you can say these are our whales — not a passing stranger.

◎ How this powers the rest of the course

Two threads start here. (1) Identity: coda rhythm is how you'll later confirm a detection belongs to the resident clan rather than a transient male or another group. (2) Structure: the multi-pulse anatomy you saw in §1 is the raw material for everything quantitative — the IPI sizes the whale in T9, and the sharp broadband click is the perfect timing mark for the time-difference-of-arrival localization you build in T10–T11. Today you learned what the sound is. Next you make sure you can sample it fast enough to keep it.

What you can now say

  1. A click is made pneumatically at the phonic lips, reflected through the spermaceti organ and junk — emerging as a multi-pulse train.
  2. The IPI (inside a click) is a ruler for head/body length; the ICI (between clicks) is the rhythm.
  3. Four click types — usual, creak/buzz, coda, slow — differ by ICI, i.e. by task.
  4. A coda encodes identity in timing, not pitch; rhythm + tempo + rubato + ornamentation give a ~150-coda "alphabet".
  5. Whales sharing a coda dialect form a vocal clan; matching the Mauritius clan's dialect is how you confirm your whales.
