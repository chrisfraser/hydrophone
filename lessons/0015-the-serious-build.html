The Serious Build: Cylinder, Preamp & Potting

Combine a sensitive PZT cylinder, your preamp, and acoustically-transparent resin into one sealed unit — the near-research-grade hydrophone you'll actually moor off Mauritius.

⏱ ~a weekend (build + 24 h cure)• 💲 ~$150–300 (Project C)• ⚗ resin + ventilation required• Hands-on build
⤴ Before you start — prerequisites

This build assumes you understand the B2 preamp — the cylinder is high-impedance and needs a buffer right at the element. You also need Project C parts on the bench. Two of them are long-lead and should already be ordered: the PZT cylinder (Steminc/APC, ships overseas) and the polyurethane potting resin (Smooth-Cast 65D, hazmat-restricted air freight). If they aren't ordered yet, order now and read on while they ship.

◎ Why this is the lesson the mission rests on

Your B1 disc lets you hear the sea; it can't measure a whale. This unit can. A PZT cylinder in water-density resin records sperm-whale clicks clean and uncoloured — flat enough to calibrate, so a recorded voltage maps back to a real pressure in µPa. That's the difference between "I think I heard a whale" and a number you can defend. Build one well, then replicate it ×3–4: matched units are exactly what the localization array needs to fix a whale's position by time-of-arrival.7

2The element: a PZT cylinder, not a disc

Swap the flat disc for a PZT cylinder — e.g. the Steminc 25×22×12 mm tube (~$49), which resonates up at ~37 kHz, far above the band you care about.5 Three wins follow from the shape. It senses omnidirectionally in the horizontal plane (a moored unit hears a whale from any bearing). Its larger electrode area gives higher capacitance, which lowers the source impedance and makes it easier to buffer with low noise. And with resonance pushed up into the ultrasonic, its usable response is wide and flat instead of a single ringing peak.1 Play with the difference below.

3Why the potting must be “acoustically invisible”

Here's the idea that separates a real instrument from a sealed lump. Sound crosses a boundary cleanly only when the two materials share an acoustic impedance Z = ρ·c (density × sound speed). Seawater sits near 1.5 MRayl. Wrap your element in ordinary hard epoxy — dense and stiff, a very different Z — and the mismatch reflects and absorbs sound at the surface, worst at high frequencies. You've built a muffler.6

Smooth-Cast 65D is a semi-rigid polyurethane with a specific gravity of 1.05 g/cc — almost exactly seawater's density.3 Its impedance lands close to water, so sound passes through it as if it were barely there: the resin is acoustically transparent.4 The trade is a touch of lower output than a thin-dipped disc (no resonant peak to lean on) in exchange for more than double the usable frequency response — flat, honest, calibratable. That is the right trade for a measurement instrument.4

★ One mental model

Dipped epoxy = a wall around the element. Water-density urethane = a window. The cylinder still does the sensing; the potting's only job is to keep the water out without the water ever noticing it's there.

4Build it: cylinder → preamp → mould

Everything electrical happens before a drop of resin is mixed. Get it working and quiet in air first; once it's potted, every fault is buried in solid urethane forever.

  1. Solder the cylinder to the preamp — short and fast
    Inner electrode → preamp signal input; outer electrode → ground/shield. Keep leads as short as possible (the high-impedance node is an antenna) and the iron quick — overheating depoles the ceramic just like it cooks a disc.
    Tip: mount the preamp (e.g. the OPA1642 Hi-Z buffer of the Gladys board) right at the element so almost no high-impedance wire is exposed.2
  2. Bench-test in air — the last chance
    Power the preamp, plug into your recorder, tap and rub the cylinder, listen for hum. This is the final moment to fix a cold joint, a swapped lead, or a ground problem.
    Tip: if it hums now it will hum forever — chase it down before the mould.
  3. Mount & suspend in the 3D-printed mould
    Seat the cylinder + preamp in the printed mould so they hang centred, not touching the walls — you want resin all around. The cable exits through the mould's gland; strain-relieve it there so a tug never reaches the solder.
    Tip: the Gladys mould is designed to suspend the element and board while you pour around them — print it (or use the kit's) rather than improvising.1
  4. Mould-release everything
    Coat the inside of the mould with release agent so the cured unit pops out. Double-check the cable seal at the gland — that entry is the #1 leak path on the finished hydrophone.
    Tip: dry-fit and rehearse the pour with the mould empty. You will not have time to think once the resin is mixing (Section 5).
  5. Pot it — then cure fully before water
    Run the 2.5-minute pour sequence below. Then let it cure: 65D is demould-hard in ~15 minutes, but give it a full 24 h cure before it ever touches water. Re-test in air, then leak-test in fresh water before the sea.
    Tip: uncured urethane and water do not mix — immerse early and you get a soft, blushed, leaky cast. Patience here protects the whole build.

5The 2.5-minute pour — pre-flight, then go

Smooth-Cast 65D has a pot life of about 2.5 minutes and is fully mixed gel-to-solid within ~15.3 That's ~150 seconds of working time — there is no time to fetch, measure or think once it's mixing. So the whole job is won before you crack the resin: stage everything, then move. Tick each item as it's ready; mix only when the list is all green.

  • Element suspended in the released mould — cylinder + preamp centred, cable through the gland, strain-relieved.
  • Air-test passed — it works and is hum-free right now. Last chance confirmed.
  • Parts A & B pre-measured — 1A:1B by volume, in separate cups, ready to combine.
  • Mixing cup + stir stick in hand — plus a scrap surface to scrape the cup against.
  • Degas plan primed — vacuum chamber ready, OR a vibration/tap surface to float bubbles out.
  • PPE on, bench ventilated — nitrile gloves, respirator, fume extraction running (Section 5 warning).
  • Timer + overflow tray set — you have ~150 s; a tray catches the spill so it never reaches you.

All green? Now run the clock — the order of operations matters because every second spent is working time spent:

The pour, against the 2.5-minute pot-life clock. Practise it dry first.
ClockDo exactly this
0:00Combine A + B and stir — ~20–30 s, scraping cup sides and bottom so no unmixed resin remains.
0:30Pour slowly down the mould wall — never straight onto the element. A thin stream sheds bubbles instead of folding them in.
1:00Degas — pull a vacuum, OR tap/vibrate the mould so trapped air rises and breaks at the surface.
1:45Top off & final tap — bring the level up, settle the surface. The resin is getting thick now.
2:30Hands off — pot life ends, the resin gels. Anything not done is done.
15:00Demould-hard — but do not rush it into water.
24 hFull cure — only now air-test, leak-test, then deploy.
⚠ Urethane safety — this resin is not the friendly part
  • Isocyanate vapour. Polyurethane resins release isocyanates while mixing/curing — respiratory and skin sensitizers that can sensitise you permanently. Mix only with good ventilation or extraction, wear a respirator and nitrile gloves, keep skin covered.3
  • Degassing under vacuum means glassware under pressure differential — use a proper chamber, eye protection, and never put your face over it.
  • Cure before water, always. Immersing an uncured cast traps blush and softness into the acoustic path — it ruins the build and can leach. Full 24 h cure, then the sea.
  • The connector is not the element. Only the potted head is waterproof; keep the dry end and its power well clear of the water.

⌁ Why "flat" is the whole point here

A resonant disc peaks then rolls off above a few kHz — and sperm-whale clicks carry energy out to ~25 kHz, so a resonant element quietly throws away the signal you came for. The cylinder's flat, omnidirectional response is what keeps the click intact. (A cheaper route to "flat" also exists: a silicone-potted electret capsule — WM-61a / Primo EM172 — though it's lower-output and not depth-rated. per Zach Poff ↗)

What you can now do

  1. Choose the cylinder: omnidirectional, higher-capacitance, flat into ultrasound — a measurement element, not a resonant toy.
  2. Seal the preamp inside, millimetres from the element, so the high-impedance node never sees an antenna of wire.
  3. Pot in water-density urethane (65D, SG 1.05) for acoustic transparency — slightly lower output, far flatter response.
  4. Beat the 2.5-minute clock: stage everything, pour down the wall, degas, then full-cure before water.
  5. Replicate it ×3–4 — matched units are the building blocks of the localization array.
