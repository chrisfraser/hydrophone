Phase 3 · Build the instrument · B1

Your First Underwater Ear

Solder a $5 piezo disc, waterproof it, drop it in the water — and actually hear the sea. A real instrument you made, in an afternoon.

⏱ ~1 afternoon• 💲 ~$20–40• 🔌 soldering iron required• Hands-on build
⤴ Before you start — this is a Phase 3 build

In the course path this comes after the Phase 1 theory and after you've ordered parts. You can read along any time, but to actually build you'll need Project A parts on the bench — order them early, since shipping is the slow part. Skimming this first to know what to buy is a perfectly good reason to be here.

◎ Today's win — and its honest limits

In one sitting you'll build a working hydrophone and hear boat props, rain, maybe the frying-bacon crackle of snapping shrimp. But be clear: this cheap disc is a learning tool, not a science instrument. It's resonant, uncalibrated and bass-rolled — perfect for practising the real skills (soldering fragile ceramic, sealing a leak-free cable entry, killing hum, the impedance trick), useless for measuring a whale. Master it now and the serious $200 build later will feel familiar.4

1What you'll gather

Everything here is cheap and substitutable. Buy a multipack of piezo discs — you will cook one or two with the soldering iron while learning, and that should never end the session.1 Tap each item once you have it; the tally tracks what's left to buy.

  • Piezo disc multipack — 27 mm brass-backed buzzer discs (×10). Ceramic centre = signal, brass rim = ground. ~$5
  • Shielded mono cable — thin coax or mic cable (core + braid). Or cut an old mic lead in half — nearly free. ~$8
  • Connector to match your recorder — 3.5 mm TS for mic-in, or ¼″ TS for a Hi-Z instrument input (best bass). ~$2
  • Solder + flux — thin rosin-core; low-wattage iron. Lead-free is kinder for a learning build. Usually already owned. ~$6
  • Waterproofing — a can of Plasti Dip or a stick of two-part plumber's/marine epoxy putty. ~$10
  • Copper foil tape (optional) — shields the disc against mains hum. Cheap insurance. ~$6
  • Heat-shrink (optional) — adhesive-lined, for strain-relieving the cable entry. ~$2
⤳ The lazy shortcut (totally fine)

Want to hear good underwater audio today without soldering? The Aquarian H2a (~$170) or H2d (~$229) has a built-in buffer and plugs straight into a recorder.16 Buying one is a legitimate way to learn what "good" sounds like while you practise building. But build at least one yourself — the skills are the point.

2Why a bare piezo sounds thin — and the one-move fix

This is the single most important idea in the lesson, and the thing that confuses everyone. A piezo isn't a simple voltage source — electrically it behaves like a tiny voltage source in series with about 15 nF of capacitance.3 That capacitor and your recorder's input resistance form a high-pass filter: plug into a low-impedance input and the bass is simply thrown away before it's recorded. Feed the same disc a high-impedance input and the lows survive.

Play with it. Pick where you plug in and watch the corner frequency move — everything to the left of the line is being cut.

🎚 Toy 1 — Why it sounds thin: the impedance high-pass

A 27 mm disc ≈ 15 nF. The recorder's input resistance sets the corner frequency fc = 1 / (2π·R·C). Below fc, bass falls away. Watch what a phone input does to your sound versus a Hi-Z instrument input.

—
★ The cheat that needs no preamp

For your first recording you don't have to build anything: terminate in a ¼″ plug and use a Hi-Z "instrument" input (on a USB interface, a Zoom recorder, or a guitar amp). It's already ~0.5–1 MΩ, so it "borrows" the impedance match and you get full, bass-present sound.3 The element never needs power — only a preamp does. We build the proper Alex Rice JFET preamp in Lesson 6; today, just borrow a Hi-Z input.

3Build it (solder first, seal second)

The golden rule: get it working in air before you waterproof anything. Once it's sealed, every fault is buried in rubber.

  1. Strip & prep the cable
    Expose the inner core (signal) and twist the braid/foil shield into one lead (ground). Slip a piece of heat-shrink onto the cable now, ready for later.
    Tip: keep the unshielded stub between cable and disc as short as possible — that bare run is where hum sneaks in.9
  2. Identify the terminals
    On the disc, the ceramic centre = signal, the brass rim = ground. Tin both wires first so the iron touches the ceramic only briefly.
    Tip: confirm before you solder — swapping these gives a non-working or hum-prone result.2
  3. Solder the element — fast
    Core → ceramic centre; shield → brass rim. Use a low-wattage iron and be quick: too much heat depoles the ceramic and kills sensitivity (it burns off the ceramic's binder).
    Tip: a dead disc after soldering was probably cooked — grab a fresh one and work faster. This is why you bought a multipack.1
  4. Strain-relieve the joint
    Anchor the wires to the rim with a dab of superglue or epoxy so the fragile solder joint never takes cable strain.
    Tip: the joint should be electrically connected but mechanically relieved — the glue takes the pull, not the solder.9
  5. Shield against hum (optional, worth it)
    Back the disc with copper foil tape and connect that foil to the cable shield/ground. This Faraday-cages the element against 50/60 Hz mains buzz.
    Tip: an unshielded high-impedance disc is literally an antenna for mains hum.11
  6. Terminate the connector
    Other cable end → your plug: tip = signal (core), sleeve = shield/ground. (For a TRS plug used as mono, tie ring to sleeve.)
    Tip: choose ¼″ TS to reach a Hi-Z input — that's your best no-preamp bass (Toy 1).
  7. BENCH-TEST in air — before sealing
    Plug in, closed-back headphones, moderate gain. Tap the disc (loud thumps), rub it (scratchy roar), watch the meters jump. Listen for hum now, while it's still fixable.
    Tip: thin-but-present on a tap is not a fault — that's the impedance high-pass. Nothing on a hard tap is a fault — fix the joint.
  8. Waterproof the head
    Seal the cable entry with hot glue first, then apply your chosen waterproofing (Section 4) in thin coats. Overbuild the cable-entry seam; go thin everywhere else.
    Tip: more coating = more waterproof but less sensitive. Resist piling it on — thin and pinhole-free wins.7
  9. Re-test, then leak-test in a bucket
    Re-run the tap test (it'll sound more muffled — expected). Then submerge for 10–20 min, watching for dropout or crackle. Add washers (isolated from the element) so it sinks and hangs straight.
    Tip: hum only underwater → bad ground/shield joint. Level swells then dies → water reached the ceramic; dry a day, recoat the seam.

4Choose your waterproofing — the trade-off

Every method trades acoustic transparency (does it pass sound cleanly, or deaden it?) against how forgiving it is for a beginner. The gold-standard urethane potting is the most transparent and the least forgiving — so it waits for the serious build. For a first ear, start in the green zone. Tap a dot to see the method.

✓ Recommended for build #1

Thin two-part epoxy, sealing mainly the wire entry (the Stanford CCRMA route) — minimal deadening, abrasion-proof.1 Or 2–3 thin coats of Plasti Dip — fastest and most forgiving, just keep coats thin so the disc isn't muffled.7 Save the acoustically-perfect Smooth-Cast 65D urethane (density ≈ water) for the real instrument — its 2.5-minute pot life is no place to learn.8

⚠ The one rule: never feed +48 V phantom to a bare piezo

Phantom power pushes +48 V DC down an XLR expecting a balanced load. A bare, unbalanced piezo takes that DC asymmetrically — it can stress or burn out the element, and at best dumps a big DC offset on it. Phantom does nothing useful for a bare piezo anyway.6 Two consequences:

  • For a bare disc, use a TRS / instrument / Hi-Z input — phantom only ever rides on XLR, so this sidesteps the hazard entirely.18
  • Only ever apply phantom to a buffer designed for it (the Alex Rice preamp, the $35 JLI P48, or Aquarian PA6) — and such buffers include DC-blocking caps, without which a phantom-charged piezo floats at ~25 V and discharges with a speaker-and-ear-damaging bang.5

6What you'll actually hear

Manage expectations and you'll be delighted instead of disappointed. The loudest thing you record is almost always yourself — a hydrophone is essentially a contact mic, so cable handling and the unit knocking the bucket boom hugely. Hold still, tie down the cable, and A/B against total stillness to find the real waterborne sound.

You'll likely hear
  • Air bubbles — instant confidence it hears through water.
  • Boat engines & props — a low throb that rises and falls; the first truly satisfying "real" sound.
  • Rain on the surface — a broadband sizzle; raindrop sizes sound wildly different (NASA uses this to gauge rainfall).12
  • Snapping shrimp (coastal) — a frying-fat crackle; one snap hits ~220 dB and lasts ~1/10,000 s.13
You won't get (yet)
  • Guaranteed whales/dolphins — location-dependent; don't expect them in a harbour test.
  • Flat, calibrated response — the disc rings and rolls off; great for listening, useless for measuring levels.4
  • Deep deployment — shallow only (bucket → bath → pool → harbour at a few metres).

7When it misbehaves

Tap to expand the symptom you're seeing.

No signal at all — meters dead even on a hard tap dead
Likely cause: cold/broken solder joint, snapped conductor, swapped/open connector, a heat-killed cracked ceramic, or the recorder on the wrong/unpowered input.
Fix: continuity-check the cable and joints end to end; reflow quickly; confirm core→centre and shield→rim; verify the input is enabled. A fresh disc that still gives nothing on tap was likely cooked while soldering — start with a new one and work fast.
Loud hum / buzz — a 50/60 Hz tone or rasp hum
Likely cause: the high-impedance piezo and its lead act as an antenna — missing/unconnected shield, a long unshielded stub, ungrounded foil, or a ground loop.
Fix: wrap the element in grounded copper foil tied to the cable shield; use proper shielded cable and keep the bare stub short; run the recorder on battery; move away from mains wiring, chargers, dimmers and LED supplies. A balanced preamp cures it on long runs.
Very thin / quiet / tinny — no bass thin
Likely cause: impedance mismatch (Toy 1). The ~15 nF disc into a low-impedance input is a high-pass filter that strips the lows.
Fix: drive a high impedance (~1 MΩ+): a Hi-Z/instrument input or a buffer. Keep the cable short. EQ can't fully recover it — boosting the missing bass also boosts hum.
Distortion / clipping — crunchy on loud events clip
Likely cause: gain too high. Piezos put out surprisingly hot transients — a tap or a nearby snap overloads the input.
Fix: turn down gain/level, engage a pad if available, back off from the loud source. A piezo usually needs less gain than a normal mic, not more.
Fades / crackles during a soak — water ingress leak
Likely cause: a pinhole or thin spot, or (most often) the cable-entry seam wicking water in to short the ceramic.
Fix: dry thoroughly for a day+, add a coat over the seam and thin areas, reinforce the cable entry with hot glue/epoxy under the coating. Re-tap-test, then re-soak before the field. Permanently weak after a flood → corroded disc; rebuild with a fresh element.

8Stay safe

⚠ Three things that actually matter
  • Lead — ingestion is the real risk. Leaded solder and PZT ceramic (>60% lead oxide) are dangerous if they reach your mouth. Don't eat/drink/smoke at the bench; wash hands and arms after; never grind bare PZT. Keep away from children and pregnant people; prefer lead-free solder.1415
  • Fumes. Rosin-flux smoke and epoxy/urethane vapours irritate lungs and skin and can sensitise you permanently — ventilate, use a small fume extractor, wear gloves and eye protection.17
  • Phantom & water. See the §5 warning. And remember the preamp/connectors are not waterproof even when the element is — keep them dry, especially in seawater.

★ Field note — for whales specifically

A cheap disc isn't only "uncalibrated" — it's resonant, which both boosts output for weak signals (handy) and rolls the response off above a few kHz — right where sperm-whale click energy lives (~5–25 kHz). So for actually capturing clicks you'll want a flatter element: the PZT cylinder of B3, or a silicone-potted electret capsule. Damping a disc (a felt pad, or bonding it to a stiff metal backing) flattens it too. And note the Plasti-Dip here is for shallow, short-term use — it peels after days submerged; real at-sea work needs B3's urethane potting. (per Zach Poff's hydrophone reviews ↗)

What you can now do

  1. Build a working piezo-disc hydrophone — solder centre→signal, rim→ground, fast, then seal.
  2. Explain why a bare piezo sounds thin (15 nF + low-Z = high-pass) and fix it with a Hi-Z input.
  3. Pick a waterproofing method by its ease/transparency trade-off — and keep coats thin.
  4. Never feed +48 V phantom to a bare piezo; route it to a buffer instead.
  5. Know what's real (boats, rain, shrimp) vs what this disc can't do (calibrated, deep, guaranteed whales).
