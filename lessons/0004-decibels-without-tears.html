Decibels Without Tears: the 1 µPa World

You keep seeing “230 dB” and “−180 dB re 1 V/µPa” thrown around. One half-hour here and every one of those numbers becomes something you can actually use.

⏱ ~10 minutes• 🧮 A little arithmetic, no calculus• Math you'll reuse everywhere
◎ Why this lesson

Every detection decision you will ever make — can I hear a whale at 5 km? — is decibel arithmetic. The sonar equation you build in T7 is just adding and subtracting decibels. So the decibel is the currency of this whole course, and right now it's a foreign currency. By the end of this page you'll read it as fluently as dollars — and you'll know the one trap (air vs water) that quietly wrecks beginners' numbers.

1A decibel is a ratio, written on a stretchy ruler

Here is the whole idea in one breath: a decibel doesn't measure a sound, it measures how many times bigger one sound's pressure is than a reference. It is a logarithmic ratio — a way of squashing enormous spans of numbers onto a short, readable scale.1

Why bother? Because the pressures in the sea are absurd. A faint sound and a sperm-whale click can differ by a factor of a hundred billion. Writing “100,000,000,000 times louder” is hopeless; writing “+220 dB” is not. The logarithm turns a chain of zeros into a tidy two- or three-digit number. That's the entire trick — decibels are a compression scheme for loudness.

⌗ “dB” alone means nothing

A decibel is always a ratio to something. Underwater, that something is a pressure of 1 micropascal (1 µPa) — a millionth of a millionth of normal atmospheric pressure, an almost absurdly quiet anchor point.2 When you see a level written in full it carries its reference with it: “dB re 1 µPa”. Drop the “re 1 µPa” and the number is just a floating digit with no meaning — like saying “it costs 230” without a currency.

2The one formula: SPL = 20·log₁₀(p / pref)

To turn a pressure p into a sound pressure level in decibels, you take its ratio to the reference, take the base-10 logarithm, and multiply by 20:2

SPL = 20 · log₁₀( p / pref ) SPL in dB re 1 µPa  ·  p = the sound pressure  ·  pref = 1 µPa underwater

Two things to notice and never forget. First, the 20 (not 10): sound pressure is what a hydrophone measures, and pressure goes in with a factor of 20. (The factor 10 is for power or intensity — a different quantity. Hydrophones read pressure, so for us it's always 20.) Second, because it's a ratio, 0 dB is not silence — it's the point where p = pref, i.e. the sound pressure exactly equals 1 µPa. Quieter sounds give negative decibels, which is perfectly normal and exactly why hydrophone sensitivity is a negative number like −180 dB re 1 V/µPa.2

★ Worked example — a number into dB, and back out

Suppose a sound's pressure is one million times the reference (p / pref = 106). Then SPL = 20·log₁₀(106) = 20·6 = 120 dB re 1 µPa. Going the other way is just the reverse: a level of 120 dB means a pressure ratio of 10(120/20) = 106 — a million times the reference again. The log and the “×20” undo each other. The converter below does exactly this both ways, instantly.

3Two rules of thumb that replace the calculator

You almost never need the full formula in your head. Two shortcuts cover most of the work, because both come straight out of the logarithm:

  • +6 dB ≈ ×2 pressure. Every 6 dB you add is (very nearly) a doubling of sound pressure — because 20·log₁₀(2) = 6.02 dB. Add 6 again, you've doubled again (×4 total, +12 dB).
  • +20 dB = ×10 pressure. Every 20 dB is exactly a tenfold change, since 20·log₁₀(10) = 20 dB. So +40 dB is ×100, +60 dB is ×1000, and so on.

With just these you can sanity-check almost anything. A click that arrives 40 dB above the noise floor is 100× the pressure of that noise — comfortably heard. Drop the level by 6 dB (move twice as far in the spherical-spreading near field) and you've halved the pressure. This is the language the whole mission is spoken in.

4The trap: in-water dB are not in-air dB

This is the single most expensive mistake a newcomer makes, so read it twice. The reference pressure is part of the unit — and water and air use different references. Underwater it's 1 µPa; in air it's 20 µPa.1 Those two anchors are 26 dB apart all by themselves, because 20·log₁₀(20) = 26 dB.3

So a “120 dB” sound in water and a “120 dB” sound in air are not the same loudness — they're measured from different starting lines. (And that 26 dB isn't even the whole story: the two media also carry sound very differently. The honest rule is simpler than the physics.)3

⚠ The one rule to memorise

Never compare an underwater dB to an in-air dB directly. When someone says a whale click is “louder than a jet engine,” they're usually comparing a dB-re-1-µPa number to a dB-re-20-µPa number — apples to oranges, off by at least 26 dB before you even get to the physics. In this course everything is dB re 1 µPa unless it explicitly says otherwise. Keep your numbers in the water and you will never be caught out.

That's also why source level carries its full address: dB re 1 µPa @ 1 m. The “@ 1 m” pins where it was measured (back-referred to one metre from the animal), and the “re 1 µPa” pins the underwater reference. A sperm whale's usual click sits near 230 dB re 1 µPa @ 1 m4 — and now you can read that string completely: a pressure ratio of about 1011.5 (over a hundred billion) above 1 µPa, at one metre. The loudest animal on Earth, in a unit you now own.

What you can now say

  1. A decibel is a logarithmic ratio to a reference — a compression scheme for huge spans of loudness.
  2. SPL = 20·log₁₀(p / pref), with the 20 because hydrophones read pressure.
  3. The underwater reference is 1 µPa; “0 dB” is that reference, not silence, and negative dB are normal.
  4. Rules of thumb: +6 dB ≈ ×2 pressure, +20 dB = ×10 pressure.
  5. In-air dB use a 20 µPa reference (26 dB off) — never compare water and air dB directly.
