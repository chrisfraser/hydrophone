Build & Deploy the Array

You can build one calibrated ear. The mission needs four of them, hung in the right places, with their clocks locked together — a real array you can lower off the western slope and use to pin Irène's group.

◎ Why this lesson — the payoff

Every prior lesson built toward a single object: a rig that can hear a sperm whale click and tell you where the whale is. One hydrophone gives you a sound. An array gives you a position. This is the physical instrument that does the thing the whole course promised — moored off the western slope of Mauritius, listening for Irène's group, and converting their clicks into points on a chart.3

1How many ears, and where to hang them

You proved the geometry in T11: each hydrophone pair draws a hyperbola, and the pairs cross at the whale. The ladder of how many ears you need is fixed by that geometry, not by budget.1 Two sensors give an ambiguous bearing; three give a position on the surface plane; four well-spread sensors give a true 3-D fix (x, y and depth), and a fifth removes the last mirror-image ambiguity.7 For pinning a deep-diving clicker you want at least four, distributed in all three dimensions.

Where you put them matters as much as how many. Close-together sensors draw sloppy, nearly-parallel hyperbolae that a tiny timing error smears across the sea; spread them far apart and the same error pins a crisp point. PAMGuard's rule is blunt: arrays should be spaced as widely as possible, with the localization excellent inside the array's footprint and usable out to a few times its width.2 The working template for exactly our mission is the Skarsoulis et al. (2022) observatory: three hydrophones moored ~1–2 km apart, localizing sperm whales in 3-D out to ~7 km by combining pairwise TDOAs with each click's surface-reflected echo as a virtual fourth arrival.3 That is the shape — a kilometre-scale, wide-aperture array — you are building toward.

2Two ways to wire it: one cable, or many buoys

There are two honest architectures, and the choice drives everything else — cost, how wide you can spread, and how hard the clocks are to tame.

① One cabled multichannel recorder

All elements run down cables into a single deck — e.g. a Zoom F6: 6 XLR inputs, up to 192 kHz, 32-bit float, per-channel +48 V phantom.4

  • Sync is free. Every channel shares one ADC clock — the recording is sample-synchronous with zero extra work.
  • Cable limits aperture. Spread is capped by how much cable you can run and moor; a kilometre of multicore is heavy, costly and lossy.
  • Powers phantom buffers down the same cable; one box to recover.

② Several autonomous nodes

Each element is its own self-contained logger — e.g. a HydroMoth: standalone, up to 384 kHz, AA-powered, case rated ~30–60 m.5

  • Cheap to spread wide. No cable between nodes, so the aperture is limited only by your boat — drop them kilometres apart. Drifting two-element rigs already detect sperm whales this way.6
  • You must time-align them afterward. Each node keeps its own clock, and independent clocks drift apart (see §3).
  • Recover and download every node separately; battery life caps the deployment.
★ The trade in one line

A cabled multichannel rig buys you perfect timing and pays in limited spread; autonomous nodes buy you unlimited spread and pay in the timing problem. For a first array within a budget, a 4–6-channel cabled recorder is the simpler, safer summit; go to autonomous nodes only once you need a wider aperture than cable can give — and have a sync plan.

3The hard part: time synchronization

This is the idea that separates a working array from a pile of recordings. Localization is built entirely on TDOA — the difference in arrival time of the same click at two hydrophones, typically tens of microseconds to a few milliseconds. If your two recorders don't agree on what time it is, that disagreement adds straight onto every TDOA, and a wrong TDOA is a wrong hyperbola.

The numbers are unforgiving. A modest clock offset of 1 ms, multiplied by c ≈ 1500 m/s, corrupts the range-difference by ~1.5 m; a 2 ms drift is ~3 m of pure error before any acoustics — and T11 showed how a glancing array geometry then multiplies that into tens or hundreds of metres of position error.2 Worse, cheap oscillators drift continuously, so even a perfect start-of-deployment sync slides out of alignment over hours.

  • One multichannel recorder = solved for free. All channels are sampled by one converter off one clock, so they are inherently sample-aligned. This is the single biggest reason to start cabled.
  • Independent recorders = the work begins. Each node must be stamped against a shared reference — a GPS receiver delivering a one-pulse-per-second (PPS) tick to every logger, or a periodic known signal — and then you still apply post-hoc clock-drift correction, fitting and removing each node's slow offset in software.8
  • Log the truth, not the plan. Record each node's actual sample-clock against UTC at start and end; researchers chasing unambiguous 3-D fixes treat this clock bookkeeping as a first-class part of the rig.9
⚠ Clocks lie — and the fix won't tell you

A timing error doesn't crash anything; it quietly moves your whale. The array will hand you a confident position that is simply wrong, and nothing in the output flags it. If you choose autonomous nodes, your synchronization scheme is not an accessory — it is the experiment. Prove it on land (clap test, two nodes, known offset) before you trust it at sea.

4The deployment hardware — getting it into the water

An array is also a mooring problem. Off the western slope of Mauritius the water is deep fast, so every element has to be held at a known place and brought home again. Be honest with yourself: this is the budget-stretching tier, and the rigging, boat time and lost-gear risk are real.

  • Floats & weights. Each hydrophone hangs on a vertical line between a sub-surface float (holds it upright, off the bottom) and an anchor weight (pins the position). A surface buoy marks it for recovery.
  • Mooring line. Enough rated line for slope depth plus scope, plus a recovery method — a surface buoy or an acoustic/galvanic release.
  • Waterproof bulkhead connectors. Wet-mateable or potted bulkhead connectors where cable enters any housing — the join is the usual leak. The element is sealed; the connectors and recorder are not.
  • Log every GPS position. You must know the geometry to invert it: record each deployed hydrophone's GPS fix (and depth on the line). An unknown sensor position is an unknown focus — the hyperbolae have nowhere to anchor, and the localization is impossible.8

Use the checklist as a pre-deployment gate. Tap each item once it's ready; the tally tracks what's still to source. Full parts and suppliers are under Project D.

  • ≥4 matched, calibrated hydrophones — B3 build replicated ×4, each B4-calibrated so their sensitivities agree. ~$600
  • Multichannel recorder or synced nodes — one Zoom F6-class deck (≥96 kHz, all channels at once), or HydroMoth-class loggers + a sync plan. ~$450
  • GPS + time reference — for logging element positions, and PPS time-stamping if you went autonomous. ~$60
  • Mooring line, anchors & floats — rated for slope depth; sub-surface float + weight per element, surface marker. ~$120
  • Waterproof bulkhead connectors — wet-mateable or potted at every housing entry; the #1 leak point. ~$80
  • Boat, crew & recovery plan — small-boat deployment over deep water; know how each unit comes back. varies
  • Land sync + leak test — clap-test the timing and bucket-soak every housing before it ever leaves the dock. free

What you can now do

  1. Size the array: at least four hydrophones, distributed in x, y and depth, spaced as widely as possible — the Skarsoulis kilometre-scale template.
  2. Pick an architecture: one cabled multichannel recorder (sync free, spread limited) vs. several autonomous nodes (spread free, sync is on you).
  3. Treat time synchronization as the experiment: single recorder = sample-synchronous; independent loggers need GPS/PPS stamping plus drift correction.
  4. Rig the deployment hardware — floats, weights, mooring line, waterproof bulkhead connectors — and log every element's GPS position, because you must know the geometry to invert it.
  5. Gate it behind a land sync + leak test before it ever goes off the western slope.
