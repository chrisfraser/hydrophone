Arrays & Hyperbolic Localization

You have a time difference from two ears. Now turn it into a place on the map — the geometry that pins a clicking whale, and the summit this whole course has been climbing toward.

⏱ ~14 minutes• 📐 Balanced math + one big toy• The summit · time → position
◎ Why this lesson — the summit

In T10 you measured a TDOA — the tiny lag between the same click reaching two hydrophones — and squeezed a bearing out of it. A bearing is a direction, not a position. This lesson finishes the job: each pair of hydrophones draws a curve of possible whale positions, and where the curves cross is your fix.6 Do this and you can do what a real observatory does — pin a sperm whale in 3-D from the sound of its clicks.4

1One time-difference draws a hyperbola

Start with the measurement you already have. A click reaches hydrophone A at time tA and hydrophone B at tB. The TDOA is Δt = tA − tB. Multiply by the speed of sound and you get not a position but a difference of distances:

Δd = rA − rB = c · Δt rA, rB = range from the whale to each hydrophone · c ≈ 1500 m/s · Δd is a fixed number for one click

Here is the whole idea. The set of every point whose distance to A minus its distance to B equals a fixed number is, by definition, a hyperbola — with the two hydrophones sitting at its foci.1 So a single TDOA doesn't give you a point. It gives you a whole curve the whale must lie on — and unlike the far-field bearing from T10, this curve is exact at any range.

⌗ Circle vs. hyperbola — don't mix them up

A constant sum of distances to two points is an ellipse; a constant distance from one point is a circle. A constant difference of distances is a hyperbola. TDOA gives you a difference — because you never measure when the whale clicked, only the gap between the two arrivals. That missing start time is exactly why you get a curve instead of a circle.

2Two curves cross → a fix

One hyperbola pens the whale onto a curve. Add a second pair of hydrophones and you get a second TDOA, a second hyperbola — and the whale must lie on both at once. Two hyperbolae generally cross at a point: that crossing is your position fix.1 Three hydrophones give you three pairs (A–B, A–C, B–C), so three hyperbolae that all pass through the same spot — a satisfying confirmation that the geometry is consistent.

Play with it below. Drag the whale, and watch every hydrophone pair redraw its hyperbola so that all of them re-cross exactly on the new position. Then add timing noise and watch the clean crossing dissolve into a smudge — your real-world error.

3How many ears do you need?

The toy makes the ladder obvious. Each well-placed hydrophone adds independent hyperbolae, and each one buys you a dimension:2

Hydrophones vs. what you can localize. "Well spread" means separated along every axis you want to resolve — including depth.2
SensorsWhat you getThe catch
2A bearing (one hyperbola)Ambiguous — a left/right mirror, and no range
3A 2-D position on the surface planeUnambiguous only for whales inside the triangle
4A 3-D fix — x, y and depthCan keep one mirror-image (above/below) twin
5A fully unambiguous 3-D positionThe fifth ear kills the last mirror twin

Four hydrophones, well distributed in x, y and depth, are the practical minimum for a true 3-D fix; a fifth removes the last ambiguity.3 Real research arrays do exactly this — a spaced cluster plus a small depth-separated group to nail x, y, z and resolve the mirror twin.7

★ The one geometry rule: spread them out

Where you put the hydrophones matters as much as how many. When two sensors sit close together their hyperbola is sloppy and barely tilts as the whale moves; spread them far apart and the same TDOA pins a much crisper curve. PAMGuard puts it bluntly: arrays should be spaced as widely as possible, with evenly distributed, widely separated hydrophones giving the most accurate localizations.1 Toggle the toy to 4 sensors, drag them wide, and watch the uncertainty patch shrink.

4Why your fix is fuzzy — and honest about it

In the toy, perfect timing makes the curves meet at a point. Reality never does. Three things blur the fix:

  • Timing error. Your TDOA is only as sharp as your clock. Sampling at 96 kS/s, one sample is ~10.4 µs, so an ideal TDOA is good to about ±5 µs — and at c ≈ 1500 m/s that's already ±8 mm of range-difference before any real-world noise.1 Push the slider and you are watching that error grow.
  • Poor geometry (GDOP). If the hyperbolae cross at a shallow, glancing angle, a tiny timing wobble slides the intersection a long way. PAMGuard's own worked example: a one-degree shift gives a 1.9 m range error with close sensors, but only 0.41 m once the pair is moved 10 m apart.1 Wide aperture = steep crossings = small error.
  • The sound bends. We assumed sound travels in straight lines at a single speed c. It doesn't. The sound-speed profile changes with depth, and rays curve toward slower water. So the true path is bent, the real travel time differs from the straight-line guess, and that mismatch becomes localization error.5 Serious systems ray-trace through the measured profile instead of assuming a straight line.
⚠ Two sensors can lie to you

A single pair gives an ambiguous bearing — the whale could be on either side of the array axis.2 If you ever localize from one pair and get a plausible-looking answer, remember its mirror image is equally valid. The cure is geometry: another spread pair, whose hyperbola crosses only one of the two candidates.

★ The blueprint this course climbs toward

Everything here is one real system. The Skarsoulis et al. (2022) observatory moors three hydrophones ~1–2 km apart as a large-aperture array and localizes sperm whales in 3-D out to ~6–7 km — using pairwise TDOAs plus the surface-reflected echo of each click as a virtual fourth arrival to recover depth.4 It runs off SW Crete, but it is the exact template for what you would moor off the western slope of Mauritius to pin Irène's group. The geometry on your screen is the geometry of that observatory.

What you can now say

  1. One TDOA, times the sound speed, fixes a difference of ranges — a hyperbola with the two hydrophones at its foci.
  2. Intersect hyperbolae from multiple pairs and the crossing point is your position fix.
  3. The ladder: 2 = ambiguous bearing, 3 = a surface position, 4 well-spread (in x, y, depth) = a 3-D fix, 5 = unambiguous.
  4. Spread the array as wide as possible — steep crossings shrink the error; close sensors blow it up.
  5. Fixes blur from timing error, poor geometry, and ray bending in the sound-speed profile.
