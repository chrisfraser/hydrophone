Phase 1 · Theory · T12

Tracking, the Law & the Community

You can pin a whale to a place. This closes the loop: turn one-off fixes into a track, do it legally from outside the circle, and plug into the people who already study these animals.

⏱ ~10 minutes• 🎓 The Phase 1 finale — no build• Mission: fix → track
◎ Why this lesson — closing the loop

In T11 you turned a time-difference into a place — one TDOA fix, one dot on the chart. But a dot is not a track, and a track of a named Mauritius whale is the real prize. This last theory lesson finishes the climb three ways: (1) stitch successive fixes into a path over time, (2) do it legally and ethically from outside the 100 m circle, and (3) hand your data to the small community already watching these whales.4

1From a fix to a track

One fix is a snapshot. But a sperm whale swims, turns, and dives for ~45 minutes at a time — so a useful result is a track: many fixes, in order, joined into a path. Getting there needs two moves, and the first is the hard one.

Move 1 — click-train association. A single foraging whale clicks roughly once a second; a group of 28 clicks at once, and the raw spectrogram becomes a thicket of vertical lines. Before you can track a whale you must decide which clicks are whose. You group the stream into per-animal click trains using continuity (a train's bearing and ICI drift smoothly, not in jumps) plus the whale's own fingerprints — its IPI-derived body length and its coda dialect. PAMGuard's Click Train Detector automates exactly this grouping.2

Move 2 — join the fixes in time. Once each train is one animal, you localize each click (or short batch) and connect the resulting positions chronologically. As fixes accumulate — or as your boat/array drifts and views the whale from changing angles — target-motion analysis (TMA) tightens the range and smooths the path into a real trajectory.1 A moored three-hydrophone observatory off a slope like Mauritius does this in 3-D out to ~7 km.3

🧵 Toy 1 — Stitch a tangle of fixes into two tracks

Twelve position fixes from a 30-minute window — two whales, criss-crossing. Raw, they're an ambiguous cloud. Press associate and the click-train fingerprints (each whale's IPI/body length) sort the dots into two clean tracks. This is the move that turns dots into a path.

⌗ Association is the bottleneck, not geometry

The hyperbolic math from T11 is exact — but it assumes you already know the same click hit each hydrophone. In a chorusing group that assumption is the whole problem. The IPI body-length ruler and the coda dialect are what let you say "these clicks are Irène, those are her calf" — turning one tangled recording into named, trackable individuals.4

2The law — and why a hydrophone is the ethical tool

Mauritius protects its resident whales with two concentric rings, set out in the Tourism Authority (Dolphin & Whale Watching) Regulations 2012.5 Know them cold; they shape every legal way of getting near a whale:

  • Inside 100 m — the prohibited zone. No vessel may enter. Full stop.
  • 100–200 m — the regulated zone. Wakeless speed, approach parallel from the side (never head-on or from behind), engine in neutral when stationary, and only one boat near a group at a time.
  • Since 3 March 2020 — no swimming or diving with whales, even with a guide. It is an offence; scientific research permits are the only exception.67

Here is the quiet point that justifies this whole course: a hydrophone studies a whale from outside the circle entirely. Drop it at 300 m, sit still, touch nothing — and you can detect, size and track an animal you never disturb. The law isn't a hurdle to your mission; listening is the design the law was practically written for. Drag the boat below and watch the zones bite.

⚠ The watching rules are the floor, not a loophole

Those distances govern watching. Deploying gear, or approaching a whale deliberately for science, sits under a separate research-permit process (via the Albion Fisheries Research Centre / Tourism Authority).7 Passive listening from well outside 200 m is the cleanest path; if your plan creeps closer, talk to the locals (§3) before you launch, not after.

What you can now say

  1. A single fix is a dot; a track needs many fixes joined in time.
  2. Click-train association comes first — group clicks per animal by continuity, IPI body length and coda dialect; then target-motion analysis smooths the path.
  3. Mauritius law: 100 m prohibited, 100–200 m regulated (wakeless, parallel, one boat), and no swimming/diving with whales since 2020.
  4. A hydrophone is the ethical tool — it studies the whale from outside the circle, disturbing nothing.
  5. You don't work alone: MMCO, Longitude 181, WILDLABS, the PAMGuard list and MARMAM are where feedback and collaboration live.
