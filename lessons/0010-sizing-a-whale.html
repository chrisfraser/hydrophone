Sizing a Whale from One Click

Before you ever build an array, a single hydrophone and a single click can tell you how big the animal is — and whether you're hearing a giant male or one of Irène's females.

⏱ ~10 minutes• 🎯 One sensor, one click, one number• A little algebra
◎ Why this lesson

Localizing a whale is hard — it takes a whole array and the maths of Phase 2. But measuring a whale is easy, and you can do it with the very first hydrophone you drop off the western slope. Back in T5 you learned that a sperm whale's click isn't one bang but a train of internal pulses. This lesson turns that quirk into a ruler: read the gap between two pulses, plug it into one line of algebra, and out comes the animal's body length. Real science from a single sensor — your first genuine measurement of a wild whale.

1The win: one click is a tape measure

Here is the surprising part. The spacing of those internal pulses — the inter-pulse interval (IPI) — is stable across all of a whale's clicks and barely changes over years. It is set by the whale's anatomy, not its mood. So you do not need a long recording, a clean train, or several sensors: one click on one hydrophone is enough to pin a length to within roughly a metre.21

That makes sizing the perfect first taste of the mission. While the array-building of Phase 3 is still on the bench, you can already point a cheap disc at the slope, catch a few clicks, and answer a real biological question: how big is this whale?

2Two intervals — and the trap everyone falls into

A sperm whale's voice hides two different gaps, and confusing them is the classic beginner mistake. One is between whole clicks; the other is between pulses inside a single click. They sound similar and they are not — only one tells you the whale's size.5

The two intervals. Same word, totally different jobs — keep them straight.

⌗ Say it once and remember it forever

The ICI is the gap you hear as the whale's tick … tick … tick — about a second apart, and it tells you the click type, not the size. The IPI is a thousand times finer — a few milliseconds — and is buried inside a single tick. ICI is rhythm. IPI is the ruler. Mix them up and you'll "measure" a 1000-metre whale.

3Where the inter-pulse interval comes from

A sperm whale's enormous forehead is mostly the spermaceti organ — a tube of waxy oil running the length of the head. The whale makes its click at the front of the head (the phonic lips, or museau de singe). Most of that sound shoots forward into the sea, but a portion travels backward through the organ, bounces off an air sac near the skull, and races forward again — escaping as a slightly later, slightly quieter second pulse. Some of that reflects once more, giving a third pulse, and so on.35

So a single click leaves the head as a decaying train of pulses — call them p0, p1, p2 — each separated by the time it takes sound to make one round trip across the organ. That round-trip time is the IPI. And here is the whole trick in one sentence:

★ The physical idea

A bigger head means a longer round trip for the echo, which means a longer IPI. Since head length tracks total body length, the IPI is a direct, built-in tape measure for the whole animal.3

4The ruler: turning milliseconds into metres

Researchers calibrated this by recording whales whose true lengths were measured independently by stereo-photogrammetry, then fitting a line. The validated result — the one this course uses — is beautifully simple:1

T = 1.258 · IPI + 5.736 T = total body length in metres · IPI in milliseconds · fitted to photogrammetric lengths, r² = 0.77 (Growcott et al. 2011)

It holds up because the IPI is so steady: within one whale, repeat measurements vary by less than 1% (mean CV ≈ 0.63%), which is why a lone click is trustworthy.12 Drop a few numbers in and the scale becomes intuitive:

6The honest caveats

The toy is clean; the sea is not. Three things keep a real measurement honest:

  • On-axis vs off-axis. Pointed straight at you, the click is so dominated by its first big pulse that the later ones can be hard to see — the click looks almost "monopulsed". The pulse train is usually clearest off to the side, so the best IPIs often come from clicks the whale was not aiming at you.7
  • Measure the right pulses. A stray surface echo or the leakage pulse p0 can pose as a "pulse" and corrupt the spacing. The reliable interval is between the clean internal reflections (p1→p2); good practice is to confirm it with several clicks, since the IPI should barely move.12
  • It's a regression, not a tape. Expect roughly ±1 m, and treat the answer as "which size class" rather than a centimetre-perfect figure.1
◎ What this buys you off Mauritius

The resident animals split sharply by size: adult females average ~11 m (an IPI near 4 ms), while a roving mature male reaches ~16 m (an IPI near 8 ms) — males run up to one-and-a-half times a female's length.4 So a single IPI off the slope already tells you whether you've found a giant visiting male or one of the females of Irène's group.6 A genuine result, from one sensor, before you solder a single connector of the array.

What you can now do

  1. Tell the IPI (pulses inside a click → size) apart from the ICI (gap between clicks → rhythm).
  2. Explain the IPI as the round-trip echo time across the spermaceti organ — bigger head, longer IPI.
  3. Convert a measured IPI to length with T = 1.258·IPI + 5.736 (ms → m).
  4. Trust a single click on a single hydrophone, because the IPI is stable to under ~1%.
  5. Off Mauritius, read an IPI as female (~4 ms) vs mature male (~8 ms) — before building any array.
