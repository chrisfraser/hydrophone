Adding a Preamp

Bolt a buffer onto your bare disc and the thin, bass-light sound from B1 fills out for good — and you unlock long cable runs, lower noise, and a balanced output your recorder loves.

⏱ ~an afternoon• 💲 ~$35 ready / ~$30 DIY• 🔌 recorder with +48 V phantom• Hands-on build
⤴ Before you start — finish B1 first

In B1 you built a bare piezo ear and heard its thin, bass-light sound — and learned why: the disc is a high-impedance ~15 nF source. This lesson fixes that permanently. Put that working hydrophone on the bench along with your Project B parts: a ready buffer (or the Alex Rice parts) plus a balanced XLR cable. You'll also need a recorder with switchable +48 V phantom.

◎ Why this is the upgrade that matters

A bare disc on a short cable into a Hi-Z input is fine for a bucket test. But to detect and localize a whale you must put the sensor in the water on a long cable, far from the boat's electrical hash. The preamp is what makes that possible: it gives the piezo the high impedance it craves (so the bass survives), then hands the recorder a low-impedance balanced signal that stays clean down tens of metres of cable. That is the difference between "I think I heard a click" and a recording you can actually detect and localize whales with.

1What a preamp actually does

Electrically your B1 disc is a tiny voltage source in series with about 15 nF of capacitance.2 Hang that off anything but a very high impedance and the capacitance plus the input resistance form a high-pass filter that throws the bass away — into a 50 kΩ line input the corner sits near 200 Hz; into a 7 kΩ mic input it climbs to ~1 kHz.2 A preamp solves this from both sides at once:

  • It presents a very high input impedance to the piezo — megohms to gigohms — so the high-pass corner drops far below the audio band and the lows come back.2
  • It presents a low output impedance to the world — so a long cable and an ordinary recorder input no longer load the signal down, and hum picked up along the run is tiny next to that strong, low-impedance drive.1
  • And it lowers self-noise versus a mismatched bare disc, while a balanced output rejects common-mode hum.5

2The signal chain — and where +48 V is allowed to go

The buffer lives right at the element: high impedance facing the piezo, low impedance facing the cable. Phantom power flows back from the recorder to power it — and the buffer's DC-blocking capacitors make sure that voltage never reaches the bare disc.1

⛓ The block diagram

Keep the unbuffered, high-impedance run between disc and buffer as short as possible — that's the fragile bit. Everything after the buffer is robust, balanced and long-cable-friendly.

4Build it — wire the element to the buffer, out via XLR

The golden rule from B1 still holds: get it working in air before you waterproof anything. These steps assume a ready buffer; for the Alex Rice board the wiring is identical, you just build the board first.

  1. Re-test the bare element first
    Plug the B1 disc into a Hi-Z input and tap-test it. Confirm it still responds before you add electronics — you want a known-good starting point.
    Tip: nothing on a hard tap means a dead joint or cooked ceramic — fix that now, not after the preamp is in the way.
  2. Place the buffer at the element end
    Mount the buffer as close to the disc as you can. The short, unbuffered stub between them is the only high-impedance run left — keep it to millimetres.
    Tip: that stub is the last place hum can still sneak in; everything downstream is low-impedance and tough.1
  3. Wire the piezo into the buffer input
    Same polarity as B1: ceramic centre → signal/hot, brass rim → ground. If you fitted copper-foil shielding, tie that foil to the buffer's ground too.
    Tip: solder the element fast and low-heat — too long and you depole the ceramic, exactly as in B1.
  4. Terminate the output in XLR
    Buffer/preamp output → XLR: pin 1 = ground, pins 2 & 3 = the balanced pair. The Alex Rice drives both pins 2 and 3; a 2-wire buffer puts signal on pin 2 and ground on pins 1 and 3 (follow its docs).
    Tip: a swapped or open pin 1 is the classic cause of "no signal, loud hum" — double-check it.
  5. Power it: phantom on, set the input right
    Engage +48 V phantom on that XLR channel and set the channel to mic (not instrument/Hi-Z — the buffer is now doing the impedance job). Raise gain gently.
    Tip: no phantom = the buffer is unpowered = dead silence. This is the first thing to check if you get nothing.1
  6. Bench-test buffered, in air
    Tap the disc: it should now thump full and bassy, not thin. A/B it against the "bare" sound from the toy above — the difference is the whole point of the lesson.
    Tip: after a preamp you usually need less recorder gain, not more (see troubleshooting).
  7. Run a long cable and listen to the noise floor
    Swap in tens of metres of XLR and listen on headphones. A buffered, balanced, low-impedance output should stay quiet over a long run where a bare disc would have collapsed into hum.
    Tip: run the recorder on battery and you remove the last ground-loop path.
  8. Waterproof the head only — keep electronics dry
    Seal the element exactly as in B1. The buffer/preamp body and the XLR are not waterproof — keep them above the waterline, especially in seawater.
    Tip: a drip into a phantom-powered connector is a fast way to kill both the buffer and your recorder's input.

5The one rule, again

⚠ Phantom feeds the preamp — never the bare piezo

This is the safety rule from B1, and now it cuts the other way: now you want phantom on, but only because a buffer is in the chain to absorb it.

  • A bare, unbalanced piezo on a phantom XLR takes +48 V DC asymmetrically and floats at ~25 V; the discharge when it touches a grounded object is a speaker- and ear-damaging bang, and the DC can stress the element.1
  • A proper buffer is built to sit between phantom and element: the Alex Rice board feeds the 48 V in through 6.81 kΩ resistors and blocks DC off the disc with 220 pF film caps on its inputs, so the ceramic never sees the rail.1
  • So: only ever switch phantom on once a buffer is in the chain. Back on the bare B1 disc, keep to a Hi-Z/instrument input and leave phantom off.

6When it misbehaves

Tap to expand the symptom you're seeing.

Still humming or buzzing — even with the preamp hum
Likely cause: the short unbuffered stub between disc and buffer is too long; the disc's foil shield isn't tied to the buffer ground; a ground loop through mains; or pin 1 isn't actually grounded.
Fix: shorten the stub to millimetres; tie the element's copper foil to the buffer ground; run the recorder on battery; and let the balanced XLR do its job — its common-mode rejection is your main hum cure on long runs.
No signal at all — meters dead dead
Likely cause: phantom not switched on (the buffer is unpowered); the channel set to instrument/line instead of mic; XLR miswired (pins 2/3 swapped, or pin 1 open); or a dead joint at the element.
Fix: confirm +48 V is on that channel, set it to mic, continuity-check pins 1/2/3, and re-tap-test the bare element first to isolate the fault.
Distortion / clipping on loud taps clip
Likely cause: double gain — the buffer already adds ~20 dB and the recorder gain is high too, so hot piezo transients overload the input.
Fix: drop the recorder gain, engage a pad if you have one, and back off the loud source. After a preamp you almost always need less gain, not more.

What you can now do

  1. Explain a preamp by its two impedances: high to the piezo (bass survives), low to the cable/recorder (clean over long runs).
  2. Build it the easy way — a ready buffer (JLI P48 ~$35, Aquarian PA6): phantom in, balanced XLR out.
  3. Or build the DIY classic — the Alex Rice JFET preamp (~20 dB balanced, 20 Hz–30 kHz, DC-blocking caps, strong hum rejection).
  4. Wire element → buffer → XLR, power it with phantom, and keep the electronics dry while you waterproof only the head.
  5. Hold the rule: phantom feeds the preamp, never the bare piezo — buffered + balanced + low-Z is the signal localization needs.
