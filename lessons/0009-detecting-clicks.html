Detecting Clicks: Thresholds, TKEO & PAMGuard

You can already read a click off a spectrogram with your eye. Now teach a computer to do it automatically — the step that turns an hour of recording into a list of time-stamped clicks.

⏱ ~14 minutes• 🛠 No hardware — practise in software• A little signal math• Mission: audio → detections
◎ Why this lesson

A foraging sperm whale dives for ~45 minutes and clicks roughly once a second the whole way down — thousands of clicks in a single dive.6 Nobody marks those by hand. Automatic detection is the machine that reads a long recording and spits out a clean list of "a click happened here, and here, and here." That list — clicks with timestamps — is the raw material for everything ahead: the arrival-time differences you'll cross-correlate to localize the animal.7 No detector, no position fix.

1From eyeballing to automatic

On a spectrogram a sperm whale click is unmistakable: a crisp vertical line, loud and broadband, standing clear of the murk. Your eye nails it in a fraction of a second. The trouble is scale — a week of recording is millions of those moments, and you cannot scroll through all of them. So we hand the job to an algorithm, and the algorithm needs a rule for what counts as a click. The whole lesson is about making that rule good.

2The simplest detector — an energy threshold

The obvious rule: a click is loud, so flag any moment where the signal energy jumps above some level. Square the samples (energy ∝ amplitude²), draw a horizontal threshold line, and every time the energy pokes above it, declare a detection.1 It is one line of arithmetic and it half-works.

Half. Because the ocean is not silent. Distant shipping, breaking waves, your own mooring creaking, a snapping shrimp next door — these throw up energy too, and a dumb threshold can't tell a click from a thump. That forces a miserable trade, the central fact of all detection:

★ The detection trade-off

Lower the threshold and you catch fainter, more distant clicks — but you also let in more noise, so false alarms climb. Raise it to silence the noise and you start missing the quiet real clicks. There is no setting that is purely "better"; every threshold buys detections with false alarms, or buys silence with misses. Engineers draw this as an ROC curve — the unavoidable compromise between probability of detection and false-positive rate.1

So the raw energy threshold isn't wrong — it's just blunt. The fix is not a cleverer threshold; it's giving the threshold a cleaner signal to work on.

3TKEO — sharpening the click first

Here is the trick that makes thresholds behave. Before thresholding, run the signal through the Teager–Kaiser Energy Operator (TKEO) — a tiny three-sample formula that estimates the instantaneous "mechanical" energy of a waveform:2

ψ[x(n)] = x(n)2 − x(n−1)·x(n+1) For a tone A·cos(ωn) it returns ≈ A²·sin²(ω) ≈ A²ω² — energy weighted by frequency squared. A click is both loud (big A) and high-frequency/broadband (big ω), so ψ shoots up. Slow, low-frequency ocean rumble has tiny ω, so ψ barely registers it.

That ω² weighting is the magic. Real ocean noise is dominated by low frequencies — shipping, swell, flow noise — exactly the band TKEO suppresses, while it amplifies the high-frequency transients that clicks are made of. The result is the same click sitting far higher above the background, so a single fixed threshold now catches it cleanly. TKEO is cheap, runs in real time, and is noted as "particularly useful for detecting transient events such as clicks" that "works well even in noisy backgrounds."1 Play with it below.

🛰 Toy — Click-detector playground: raw energy vs TKEO

A noisy 4-second clip hides 5 real clicks. Move the threshold and the noise; tap the button to switch the detector between raw energy and TKEO. Markers along the top score every threshold: caught · false alarm · missed click. With raw energy a clean threshold barely exists; with TKEO almost any threshold catches all five.

—

Notice the pattern: in ENERGY mode, crank the noise up and the safe window for the threshold collapses — set it low and false alarms flood in, set it high and the faint clicks vanish. Switch to TKEO and the low-frequency floor drops away; the five clicks stand alone, and a wide range of thresholds gets all of them with zero false alarms. Same data, same threshold idea — just a sharper signal underneath it.

4PAMGuard — the tool you'll actually use

You don't have to code any of this. PAMGuard is the free, open-source (GPL) software that is the field standard for passive acoustic monitoring — "world leading software for the Detection, Classification, and Localisation of marine mammal sounds," used everywhere from research moorings to Greenpeace surveys.3 It runs the same logic you just played with, only properly, and needs no programming.

Two of its modules matter to you now:

The two PAMGuard modules that take you from audio to per-animal click lists.
ModuleWhat it does for you
Click DetectorFinds transients (your threshold + a TKEO-style energy step), then classifies them — a Basic classifier inherited from RainbowClick for high-frequency porpoise clicks, or a Frequency-Sweep classifier that suits lower-frequency whales. Each click is tested against each type in turn.4
Click Train DetectorGroups the loose pile of detections into click trains — sequences of regularly spaced clicks from one animal — using the inter-click interval. This is what separates two whales clicking at once, and what later feeds localization.5
⤳ Practise with no hardware

You can do all of this today on a laptop: download PAMGuard, feed it a free sperm-whale recording, and watch the Click Detector light up the same vertical lines you'd mark by eye — then let the Click Train Detector sort them into one whale's train. That's your dress rehearsal for the real array, long before you've soldered anything.

What you can now say

  1. Automatic detection turns a long recording into a time-stamped list of clicks — the raw material for localization.
  2. A raw energy threshold works but is blunt: every setting trades false alarms against misses (the ROC trade-off).
  3. TKEO — ψ[x(n)] = x(n)² − x(n−1)·x(n+1) — weights energy by frequency², lifting clicks above low-frequency noise so a threshold works far better.
  4. PAMGuard (free, open-source, field standard) runs this for you: the Click Detector finds & classifies, the Click Train Detector groups clicks per animal.
  5. You can practise the whole pipeline today on free recordings — no hardware required.
