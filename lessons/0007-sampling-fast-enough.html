Sampling Fast Enough: Nyquist & the Click Problem

A digital recorder only takes snapshots. Take them too slowly and a sperm whale's click doesn't just blur — it turns into a different sound entirely, and nothing can change it back.

⏱ ~10 minutes• 🧮 Light math, one hard rule• Decides which recorder you buy
◎ Why this lesson

You can already read a spectrogram (T4) and you know a sperm-whale click is broadband — energy smeared across a huge band of frequencies (T5). Now meet the hardware wall that all of that runs into. A single number on a recorder's spec sheet — its sample rate — decides whether you ever capture a click at all. Get it wrong and the loudest animal on Earth arrives as mush, irreversibly. This one rule is why we can pick your recorder before you spend a cent.

1The one rule: sample more than twice as fast

A hydrophone gives you a smooth, continuous voltage. To store it, the recorder takes samples — instantaneous snapshots of that voltage, fs of them every second. The question Nyquist answers is brutal and exact: how many snapshots per second do you need so the original wave can be rebuilt without ambiguity?

The answer is the Nyquist rate: you must sample at more than twice the highest frequency you care about.1 Two samples per cycle is the bare floor; below it, a wave can wear a disguise. Flip it around and the same rule names the highest frequency a given recorder can honestly capture — the Nyquist frequency, exactly half the sample rate:

fs > 2 × fmax    ⇔    Nyquist frequency = fs / 2 Everything below fs/2 survives intact. Everything above it is trouble — see §2.

So the arithmetic is short. Want to keep every wiggle up to 30 kHz? You need fs > 60 kHz. A 96 kHz recorder gives you a Nyquist frequency of 48 kHz — comfortably clear. A 48 kHz video recorder gives you only 24 kHz, and quietly throws the rest away.

2Aliasing: the ghost that can't be exorcised

Here's the part that bites. When you undersample, a too-high frequency doesn't politely vanish. It folds back down and reappears as a false, lower frequency — an alias — sitting right on top of your real data.1 Sample a 24 kHz tone at 32 kHz (Nyquist = 16 kHz) and it comes back as an 8 kHz ghost. You've recorded a sound the whale never made.

You have seen this: a car's wheels appear to spin backwards on film because the camera's frame rate undersamples the spokes. Same trap. And it is irreversible — once two different frequencies land on the very same string of sample dots, the file simply cannot tell them apart. The information is destroyed before it is written, so no equaliser, no clever software, no AI gets it back. The only fix is to sample fast enough in the first place.

⌗ "But doesn't a filter stop this?"

Good real recorders put an anti-alias low-pass filter in front of the converter, which throws away everything above the Nyquist frequency before sampling — so the ghost never forms. That protects the rest of your recording, but notice what it costs: the high part of the click is gone either way. Filtering trades a false signal for no signal. The only way to actually keep the click's high frequencies is the honest one — sample faster.

3So how fast for a sperm-whale click?

Now point the rule at your quarry. A usual click is broadband: its energy runs from a few hundred hertz up into the tens of kilohertz, with the bulk centred around 5–15 kHz and useful content reaching roughly 30 kHz.2 To keep all of that, your Nyquist frequency has to sit above ~30 kHz — which means fs > ~60 kHz.

That is exactly why the field has converged on ≥96 kHz as the working standard for click work. Wideband arrays built to study these animals run flat to well past human hearing and sample at 96 kHz;3 a real passive-acoustic survey in the Atlantic recorded its hydrophones at 192 kHz — and two of them at 500 kHz — then decimated down to 96 kHz for click analysis.2 Published PAM guidance simply lists a minimum sample rate per species so a system is defensible.4

What you can now say

  1. To capture a frequency faithfully you must sample at more than twice it — the Nyquist rate.
  2. The Nyquist frequency = fs/2 is the honest ceiling of any recorder.
  3. Undersampling causes aliasing: a high frequency folds into a false low one, and it is irreversible.
  4. Sperm-whale clicks reach ~30 kHz, so fs must exceed ~60 kHz — making ≥96 kHz the working standard.
  5. Sample rate is the first filter on your recorder shortlist: under 96 kHz can't record a click.
