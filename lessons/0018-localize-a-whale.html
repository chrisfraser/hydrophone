1The pipeline — five moves you already own

Localization in the field is not one big trick; it is five small moves chained together, and you have already practised every one of them. The only new skill is running them in order, on the same recording.

The end-to-end localization pipeline. Each stage is a lesson you have already done — this lesson just connects them.1

⌗ Notice what timing alone buys you

With zero noise the hyperbolae meet exactly on the whale and the fix sits dead on the truth — you never measured loudness, never knew when the whale clicked, only the tiny gaps between four arrivals. Push the noise slider and the same geometry degrades gracefully: the fixes scatter, but the track still traces the whale's path, because a wide array makes the hyperbolae cross at steep angles.2

3Doing it for real in PAMGuard

You can code this pipeline yourself, but you don't have to. PAMGuard — the free, open-source tool working acousticians use — implements every stage as a module you wire together in a data model. Here is the exact chain for sperm-whale clicks on your array:2

  1. Sound Acquisition → multichannel
    Point PAMGuard at your synchronized multichannel file (or live device) and tell it the array geometry — each hydrophone's x, y, z. The geometry is what turns timings into metres.
    Tip: get the hydrophone positions right; PAMGuard warns that position uncertainty feeds straight into every fix.2
  2. Click Detector → find the clicks
    The click detector triggers on each broadband transient across the channels — the software equivalent of the energy/TKEO detector from T8.
    Tip: add a click classifier to keep sperm-whale clicks and reject snapping shrimp and echosounder pings.
  3. Click Train Detector → one whale at a time
    Group the click stream into per-animal click trains by continuity, ICI and the whale's IPI/coda fingerprint — so you localize a whale, not a chorus.4
    Tip: this association step is the real bottleneck — the geometry that follows assumes you've matched the same click across channels.
  4. TDOA / large-aperture localizer → the fix
    For each click, PAMGuard cross-correlates the channels for the pairwise TDOAs and intersects the hyperboloids. With ≥4 well-spread hydrophones the output is a 3-D position.36
    Tip: for a widely spaced moored array, use the large-aperture / group 3-D localizer rather than the small-array beamformer.
  5. Map → the track
    The Map shows each fix in real time; as the whale keeps clicking, target-motion analysis tightens the range and the fixes stitch into a trajectory you can export.2
    Tip: overlay your boat's GPS so the track is in real-world coordinates you can hand to MMCO.

4How accurate is it, really — and your legal target

Be honest about what this delivers. The reference system for exactly this job — Skarsoulis et al. (2022) — moors three hydrophones ~1–2 km apart and localizes foraging sperm whales in 3-D out to roughly 6–7 km from the array, using pairwise TDOAs plus each click's surface-reflected echo to recover depth.1 That is the realistic envelope for a modest moored array: a usable 3-D fix on a clicking whale anywhere inside a few kilometres. Two things set that limit:

  • Timing. Your fix is only as sharp as your TDOAs. Sampling and cross-correlation set a floor; poor array geometry (shallow hyperbola crossings) then amplifies any timing wobble. Spreading the array wider is the cure.2
  • The sound bends. We assume a single speed c ≈ 1500 m/s and straight rays. Real ocean sound speed varies with depth and rays curve toward slower water; that refraction is the dominant error, and Skarsoulis notes the correct sound-speed profile matters because refraction significantly affects the result.51 Serious systems ray-trace through the measured profile instead of assuming a straight line.

And the target is real. Mauritius has a small, resident, individually-named sperm-whale community — fewer than 40 catalogued animals, with Irène's year-round group of roughly 28.78 A hydrophone array reaches them legally from well outside the 100 m prohibited zone: drop it in free water beyond 200 m, sit still, and you detect, size and track an animal you never disturb. The position on your screen is a real point in the sea, on a whale you could put a name to.
