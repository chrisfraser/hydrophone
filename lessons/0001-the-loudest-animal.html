Phase 1 · Theory & Foundations · T1

The Loudest Animal in the Sea

Before you build anything, meet your quarry — and find out why a hydrophone the size of your thumb can hear a sperm whale kilometres away.

⏱ ~12 minutes• 🛠 No build required — do this today• Mission: detect & localize
◎ Why this lesson

Your mission is to build a hydrophone and use it to find and track the resident sperm whales of Mauritius. Everything downstream — the element you buy, how fast you sample, how you turn two microphones into a position fix — is shaped by one fact: what these animals sound like, and how their sound travels. This lesson hands you that fact, with two toys you can play with right now.

1The whales are already there

Off the western slope of Mauritius lives a small, resident population of sperm whales — fewer than forty animals, catalogued one by one from photographs since 2011.5 At its heart is a stable matrilineal family — "Irène's group", around 28 whales — seen year after year in the same waters.4 The western continental slope is recognised as one of the most important sperm-whale breeding and feeding habitats in the entire southwestern Indian Ocean.6

This is the rare gift your mission rests on: you do not have to chase whales across an ocean. They have an address, and they are home all year. Your job is to learn to listen.

⚠ Listen, don't disturb

Mauritius law sets a 100 m no-go "prohibited zone" around a whale and a 100–200 m "regulated zone"; since March 2020 it is an offence to swim or dive with them without a scientific permit.10 A hydrophone is the perfect tool here — it lets you study a whale from far outside that circle, without touching its world. We will treat the law as a design constraint, not an afterthought.

2What a sperm whale actually sounds like

A sperm whale does not sing. It clicks — short, broadband bursts of sound it uses as biological sonar to hunt squid in the lightless deep.1 And these are not gentle clicks. A sperm whale's "usual" click is the loudest sound made by any animal on Earth, with on-axis source levels reported up to ~236 dB re 1 µPa @ 1 m.3 For comparison, that figure dwarfs a rifle shot. This loudness is your single biggest advantage.

Clicks come in types, told apart by their rhythm — the inter-click interval (ICI):

⌗ Two intervals — don't mix them up

The gap between whole clicks is the ICI — it sets the rhythm. But hidden inside a single click is a second, tiny gap: the inter-pulse interval (IPI), an echo bouncing back and forth across the whale's huge spermaceti organ. The IPI is a ruler: a longer head means a longer IPI, so one click can tell you the whale's body length.1 We'll measure it with a single hydrophone in a later lesson — your first "win" that needs no array.

3Why you can hear them from kilometres away

Sound spreads out from a whale like an expanding sphere. As the sphere grows, the same energy is smeared over a bigger and bigger surface, so the sound gets quieter with distance. The rule near the source is brutally simple — every doubling of distance costs about 6 dB (this is spherical spreading: TL = 20·log₁₀ r).7 Far out, once the sound is trapped between the sea surface and the seabed, the loss eases to ~3 dB per doubling.

Start at 236 dB and you have an enormous budget to spend on distance. That is the whole reason a cheap hydrophone works: the whale does the hard part by being deafeningly loud. The calculator below is a first, honest taste of the passive sonar equation we build properly in Lesson 10.

★ The honest caveat (and your roadmap)

That tool assumes the click points straight at you in open water. Reality is stingier: the loud click is a forward-pointing beam, so off to the side it's far quieter; rough seas raise the noise; and pinpointing a position needs the whale closer still. A real Mauritius-like observatory — three moored hydrophones ~1–2 km apart — localizes sperm whales in 3-D with useful accuracy out to about 7 km.9 That study is the blueprint this whole course climbs toward.

What you can now say

  1. Mauritius has a small resident sperm-whale population on its western slope — a fixed, year-round target.
  2. Sperm whales click; the usual click is the loudest animal sound on Earth (~230 dB re 1 µPa @ 1 m).
  3. Click types differ by ICI; in a spectrogram each click is a vertical line.
  4. Sound weakens by ~6 dB per doubling of range near the source — but the whale is so loud you can still hear it kilometres off.
  5. You'll listen from outside the 100 m zone — legal, ethical, and exactly what a hydrophone is for.
