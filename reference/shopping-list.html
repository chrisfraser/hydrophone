⏱Buy these first — the long-lead items

Shipping, not money, is the bottleneck. These take longest to arrive (specialist suppliers, overseas), so order them at the start of the theory phase and they'll be waiting when you reach the builds:

  • long lead PZT cylinder element(s) — Steminc / APC ship from the US/overseas (Project C & D).
  • long lead Polyurethane potting resin — Smooth-Cast 65D (Smooth-On), hazmat-restricted shipping (Project C).
  • long lead Multi-channel recorder — Zoom F6 / HydroMoth etc.; big-ticket, worth choosing early (Project D, but used everywhere).
  • order early Piezo disc multipack & connectors — cheap but often from overseas marketplaces (Project A).
  • order early Preamp board or buffer — JLI P48 / Gladys kit / Aquarian PA6 (Project B & C).
✓ The one-line strategy

Do the up-front purchase plan below to your budget on day one. Install the free software immediately and practise on public whale recordings during the theory phase. By the time the parts land, you'll know exactly what to do with them.

🛠Tools — one-time, reused for every build

Standard maker kit. You may own much of it. Buy once; it serves Projects A through D.

  • Temperature-controlled soldering iron — low-wattage; a fine tip for the fragile ceramic. ~$25–50
  • Solder + flux pen — thin rosin-core; lead-free recommended for a home bench. ~$10
  • Multimeter — continuity test is the #1 build debugger. ~$15–30
  • Wire strippers + flush cutters ~$10–15
  • Helping-hands / PCB clamp ~$10
  • Heat gun — for adhesive heat-shrink. ~$15–25
  • Safety: glasses, nitrile gloves, fume extractor/fan — non-negotiable for solder/epoxy fumes & lead. ~$20–60

A–DThe projects

Each build lesson maps to a project below. Tap items you already have; the tally shows what's left to buy.

Project A · Starter piezo-disc hydrophone ~$25–40

The cheap learning ear from the build lesson. Buy a multipack of discs — you'll cook one or two while learning.

  • 27 mm piezo disc multipack (×10) — e.g. Murata 7BB-27-4 / PUI AB2746B, ~$1.30 each. fast/local ~$5
  • Shielded mono cable — thin coax/mic cable, or cut an old mic lead (≈ free). fast/local ~$8
  • ¼″ or 3.5 mm TS connector — e.g. ShowMeCables mono plug, ~$2. order early ~$2
  • Plasti Dip or epoxy putty — waterproofing (thin coats!). fast/local ~$10
  • Optional: copper foil tape + heat-shrink — hum shielding + strain relief. fast/local ~$8

Project B · Preamp upgrade ~$35–60

Fixes the thin/bass-light sound for good and lets the hydrophone drive long cables. The DIY classic is the Alex Rice JFET preamp; the no-soldering shortcut is a ready buffer.

  • JLI P48 Hi-Z Piezo Buffer — phantom-powered, no battery, ~$35. The safe, simple path. order early ~$35
  • Balanced XLR cable — to carry the buffered signal to your recorder. fast/local ~$12
DIY alternative (~$25–40): build the Alex Rice piezo preamp yourself — a low-noise JFET front-end (2SK170-class JFET, op-amp, passives, XLR). More soldering, more learning, less money. Covered in the preamp build lesson.

Project C · Serious single hydrophone ~$150–300

The instrument the mission rests on: a PZT cylinder element + preamp + acoustically-transparent potting. Order the cylinder and resin first — they're the slowest to arrive.

  • PZT cylinder element — Steminc 25×22×12 mm ~$49, or 51×45×14 mm ~$55. Omnidirectional, sensitive. long lead ~$49
  • Preamp / buffer — P48 (Project B), Alex Rice DIY, or Aquarian PA6. order early ~$35
  • Smooth-Cast 65D polyurethane — density ≈ water, acoustically transparent. Trial unit + mold. long lead ~$40
  • Shielded cable + XLR + housing bits — longer run for in-water deployment. order early ~$20
Two buy-instead shortcuts: (1) the all-in-one Gladys Hydrophone Kit (element + preamp + potting in one box — check current price); or (2) a turnkey Aquarian H2a (~$170) / H2d (~$229) — calibrated, ready to record, and a known-good reference to compare your DIY build against.

Project D · Localization array outline only

The summit: multiple synchronized hydrophones + a multi-channel recorder, to fix a whale's position by time-difference-of-arrival. Don't buy this yet — the localization lessons decide the geometry (how many elements, how far apart). This is the shape of it:

  • 3–4+ matched hydrophones — replicate Project C, or buy multiple Aquarians. (≥4 well-spread for a true 3-D fix.)
  • Multi-channel recorder — see Recorders; must sample ≥96 kHz on all channels at once.
  • Time synchronization — open question the hard part if elements aren't on one cabled recorder (GPS/PPS or clock-drift correction). We'll design this in the array lesson.
  • Deployment hardware — floats, weights, mooring line, waterproof connectors, a way to log positions.
Two architectures to weigh later: one cabled multi-channel rig (all elements → a Zoom F6-class recorder; sync is automatic, range limited by cable) vs. several autonomous nodes (HydroMoth-class recorders spread wide; cheaper to spread out, but you must time-align them afterward).

⏺Recorders — the cross-cutting big decision

The recorder serves every project, so choose it early. The hard requirement: ≥96 kHz sampling to capture sperm-whale click energy (why), and enough inputs for your array.

⚓Buying into Mauritius — practical notes

⌁ Plan around shipping & duty
  • Consolidate orders to cut per-parcel shipping and customs handling — buy a project's worth at once, not piece by piece.
  • Budget for import duty + VAT on electronics; it can add 15%+ on top of shipping. Factor it into the tiers above.
  • Hazmat items ship slowly — resins (Smooth-Cast), some adhesives and batteries face air-freight restrictions; order earliest.
  • Source locally where you can — soldering tools, epoxy putty, cable, weights and floats are often available on-island faster and cheaper than importing.
  • Talk to the locals. MMCO and others (see Resources → Communities) already run gear in these waters — they may know suppliers, or have kit to point you at.
