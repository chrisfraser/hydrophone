The instrument

Hydrophone

A passive underwater microphone — usually piezoelectric — that converts the pressure of a passing sound wave into a small electrical voltage. "Passive" means it only listens; it never emits sound.

Piezoelectric element

A ceramic (typically PZT — lead zirconate titanate) disc or cylinder that generates an electric charge when mechanically deformed by sound pressure. It is the sensing core of most hydrophones, and the cheap part that makes a DIY build possible.

Receiving sensitivity M dB re 1 V/µPa

How much voltage a hydrophone produces per unit of sound pressure. Always a negative number; less negative = more sensitive (−164 dB beats −180 dB). The figure you must know to turn recorded volts back into real pressure — see calibration.

Preamplifier

A buffer/amplifier placed right at the element that matches the piezo's very high impedance to a recorder's low-impedance input. Without it the signal is lost and the sound is thin and coloured. Integrated (in-sensor) preamps outperform cable-mounted ones.

Levels & the decibel world

Decibel dB

A logarithmic ratio of two quantities. Underwater sound is referenced to 1 micropascal (1 µPa), whereas air uses 20 µPa — so an in-water dB figure is not comparable to an in-air one. Roughly: +6 dB ≈ double the pressure, +20 dB ≈ ten times.

Source level SL dB re 1 µPa @ 1 m

How loud a source is, referred back to 1 m from it. Sperm whale clicks reach ~230–236 dB re 1 µPa @ 1 m — the loudest sound made by any animal.

Transmission loss TL dB

How much a sound weakens with range, dominated by geometric spreading: −6 dB per range-doubling (spherical, TL = 20·log₁₀ r) close to the source, easing to −3 dB (cylindrical, TL = 10·log₁₀ r) once the sound is trapped between surface and bottom.

Ambient noise / noise level NL

Background ocean sound — distant shipping (low frequencies), wind and breaking waves (mid/high), thermal noise (very high) — summarised by the Wenz curves. It sets the floor a signal must rise above to be heard.

Passive sonar equation

The bookkeeping that decides whether you hear a source: SE = (SL − TL) − (NL − DI) − DT, where DI is the array directivity index and DT the detection threshold. No target-strength term and only one-way TL (you listen, you don't ping). Detectable when signal excess SE > 0 dB.

Calibration

Assigning a hydrophone its true sensitivity (via pistonphone or comparison against a reference unit). Required before recorded volts can become absolute sound pressure — the line between a "microphone" and a measuring instrument.

Capturing & viewing

Nyquist rate

The minimum sampling rate to capture a signal faithfully: more than twice the highest frequency of interest. Sample too slowly and high frequencies alias into false low ones. Sperm whale clicks reach tens of kHz → ≥96 kHz is the working standard.

Spectrogram

A frequency-versus-time image of sound (time →, frequency ↑, colour = energy). Transient clicks appear as crisp vertical lines, which is why it is the primary tool for inspecting whale recordings.

The quarry — sperm whale sounds

Regular (usual) click

A sperm whale's broadband echolocation click (~300 Hz–30 kHz, most energy ~5–25 kHz, ICI ~0.5–2 s). Highly directional and forward-beamed at high frequencies; used to find prey in the deep.

Coda

A short, patterned, rhythmic sequence of clicks used socially (not for echolocation). Whales sharing coda dialects form vocal clans. The Mauritius residents have their own clan dialect.

Inter-click interval ICI

The time gap between successive clicks in a train. It distinguishes click types: usual ~0.5–2 s, creak/buzz ~5–100 ms, coda ~0.1–0.5 s, slow click ~5–8 s.

Inter-pulse interval IPI

The spacing between the internal pulses within a single click, set by sound bouncing across the spermaceti organ. A stable proxy for head — and thus body — length: T = 1.258·IPI + 5.736 (IPI in ms → length in m). Do not confuse with ICI.

Localization — the summit

Time difference of arrival TDOA

The difference in arrival time of the same sound at two hydrophones — the basic measurement behind localization. Each TDOA, times the sound speed, fixes a difference in range to the source.

GCC-PHAT

Generalized Cross-Correlation with Phase Transform — phase-whitens two signals so the correlation peak is sharp and robust under noise and reverberation. The standard way to measure TDOA (Knapp & Carter, 1976).

Hyperbolic localization

Locating a source by intersecting the hyperboloids defined by TDOAs between hydrophone pairs. Two sensors give an (ambiguous) bearing; ≥4 well-spread sensors give a true 3-D fix.

Sound-speed profile SSP ~1450–1570 m/s

How sound speed varies with depth (via temperature, salinity, pressure — nominal ~1500 m/s). It bends ray paths (rays curve toward slower water) and is the main source of localization error. A sound-speed minimum near ~1000 m forms the long-range SOFAR channel.
